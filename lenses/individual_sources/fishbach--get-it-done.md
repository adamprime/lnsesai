# Get it Done
**Author:** Fishbach

**Tags:** Motivation, Goal setting

## Summary

"Get It Done: Surprising Lessons from the Science of Motivation" by Ayelet Fishbach presents a research-backed framework for understanding motivation and achieving goals more effectively. Drawing from her extensive work as a behavioral scientist at the University of Chicago Booth School of Business, Fishbach challenges conventional wisdom about motivation and offers evidence-based strategies for sustaining drive and accomplishing objectives.

The book's central premise is that motivation is not a fixed trait but a skill that can be developed and optimized through understanding psychological principles. Fishbach argues that many common approaches to goal-setting and motivation are counterproductive, and she provides alternative strategies grounded in scientific research. Rather than relying on willpower alone, she advocates for designing systems and environments that naturally support sustained motivation.

One of the book's key insights is the importance of understanding different types of goals and how they affect our motivation differently. Fishbach distinguishes between "want" goals (intrinsically motivating activities we enjoy) and "should" goals (activities we believe we ought to do). She explains how to leverage both types effectively, showing readers how to find intrinsic motivation in necessary but unenjoyable tasks while maintaining momentum on personally meaningful objectives.

The author introduces the concept of "goal gradient effect," demonstrating how people tend to accelerate their efforts as they approach goal completion. This principle can be harnessed by breaking large goals into smaller milestones, creating multiple opportunities to experience the motivational boost that comes from nearing achievement. Fishbach also explores how framing progress differently—focusing on accomplishments versus remaining tasks—can significantly impact sustained motivation.

Another crucial framework involves understanding the role of feedback in motivation. Fishbach explains how different types of feedback affect motivation at various stages of goal pursuit. Early in the process, positive feedback about progress made can boost confidence and commitment. However, as people advance toward their goals, feedback about remaining gaps can be more motivating by highlighting the work still needed.

The book addresses common motivational pitfalls, including the planning fallacy, where people underestimate the time and effort required for tasks. Fishbach provides strategies for more realistic goal-setting and maintaining motivation when progress is slower than expected. She also tackles the challenge of competing goals, offering methods for prioritizing and managing multiple objectives without becoming overwhelmed or losing focus.

Fishbach emphasizes the social dimensions of motivation, exploring how relationships and social contexts influence goal pursuit. She discusses the benefits and potential drawbacks of sharing goals with others, the role of social support, and how to leverage accountability effectively. The book also examines how helping others can paradoxically boost our own motivation and goal achievement.

The author addresses procrastination not as a character flaw but as a predictable response to certain goal structures and environmental factors. She provides practical strategies for overcoming procrastination by changing how goals are framed, structured, and approached. This includes techniques for making distant goals feel more immediate and relevant.

Throughout the book, Fishbach integrates findings from behavioral economics, psychology, and neuroscience to explain why certain motivational strategies work while others fail. She debunks popular myths about motivation, such as the idea that visualizing success is always beneficial, showing instead that mental contrasting—imagining both success and potential obstacles—is more effective for sustained motivation.

The book's practical applications extend beyond personal goal-setting to leadership, team management, and organizational behavior. Fishbach provides insights for managers and leaders on how to motivate others effectively, create motivating environments, and design systems that support sustained performance.

"Get It Done" stands out for its scientific rigor combined with practical applicability. Rather than offering generic advice, Fishbach provides specific, research-backed strategies that readers can immediately implement. The book's significance lies in its potential to help individuals and organizations move beyond motivation myths toward evidence-based approaches that actually work. By understanding the science of motivation, readers can design more effective goal-pursuit strategies, overcome common obstacles, and maintain momentum toward their most important objectives.

## Theme Analysis

### Theme 1: Science-Based Motivation
**Explanation:** Fishbach emphasizes that effective motivation strategies must be grounded in empirical research rather than intuition or popular wisdom. She systematically debunks common motivational myths while providing evidence-based alternatives that actually work.
**Examples:** The book challenges the effectiveness of pure positive visualization, instead advocating for mental contrasting. It also examines research on feedback timing and shows how different types of feedback work better at different stages of goal pursuit.

### Theme 2: Goal Structure and Design
**Explanation:** The way goals are structured and framed significantly impacts motivation and achievement rates. Fishbach demonstrates how small changes in goal design can lead to dramatically different outcomes in terms of sustained effort and completion rates.
**Examples:** The goal gradient effect shows how breaking large goals into smaller milestones creates multiple motivation boosts. The distinction between "want" and "should" goals helps readers understand why some objectives feel energizing while others feel draining.

### Theme 3: Strategic Use of Feedback
**Explanation:** Feedback is not universally motivating; its effectiveness depends on timing, framing, and the individual's stage in goal pursuit. Understanding when and how to use different types of feedback can significantly enhance motivation and performance.
**Examples:** Early-stage goal pursuit benefits from highlighting progress made, while later stages benefit from emphasizing remaining work. The book shows how to strategically shift feedback focus to maintain optimal motivation throughout the goal pursuit process.

### Theme 4: Social Dimensions of Motivation
**Explanation:** Motivation is inherently social, influenced by relationships, accountability, and social contexts. Fishbach explores how to leverage social factors effectively while avoiding potential pitfalls of social goal-sharing.
**Examples:** The book examines when sharing goals publicly helps versus hurts motivation, how helping others can boost personal goal achievement, and strategies for creating effective accountability partnerships without undermining intrinsic motivation.

### Theme 5: Overcoming Procrastination Through System Design
**Explanation:** Procrastination is treated as a predictable response to certain goal structures rather than a character flaw. By redesigning how goals are approached and structured, individuals can systematically reduce procrastination tendencies.
**Examples:** Techniques for making distant goals feel more immediate and relevant, strategies for breaking overwhelming tasks into manageable components, and methods for creating environmental cues that support desired behaviors rather than delay them.

### Theme 6: Sustainable Long-term Motivation
**Explanation:** Unlike approaches that rely on short bursts of willpower, Fishbach focuses on creating systems and mindsets that support sustained motivation over extended periods. This involves understanding motivation as a renewable resource that can be strategically managed.
**Examples:** The book provides strategies for maintaining motivation when progress is slow, methods for recovering from setbacks without losing momentum, and techniques for finding intrinsic motivation in necessary but unenjoyable tasks.
