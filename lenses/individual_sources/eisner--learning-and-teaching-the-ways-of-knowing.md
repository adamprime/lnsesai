# Learning and Teaching the Ways of Knowing
**Author:** Eisner

**Tags:** Thinking

## Summary

"Learning and Teaching the Ways of Knowing" by Elliot W. Eisner, published as the Eighty-Fourth Yearbook of the National Society for the Study of Education (Part II), presents a groundbreaking examination of how different forms of knowledge are acquired, processed, and transmitted in educational settings. Eisner, a prominent educational theorist and advocate for arts education, challenges the traditional dominance of scientific and logical-mathematical ways of knowing in schools, arguing instead for a more pluralistic approach to understanding and learning.

The book's central thesis revolves around the concept that human beings possess multiple ways of knowing that extend far beyond the narrow confines of traditional academic subjects. Eisner contends that schools have historically privileged certain forms of knowledge—particularly those that can be easily measured and quantified—while systematically devaluing others, such as artistic, intuitive, and experiential ways of understanding the world. This educational myopia, he argues, not only limits students' intellectual development but also fails to prepare them for the complexity and richness of human experience.

Eisner introduces the concept of "forms of representation," which refers to the various symbolic systems through which knowledge is encoded, stored, and communicated. These include not only written and spoken language but also visual images, musical notation, mathematical symbols, and bodily movement. Each form of representation, according to Eisner, offers unique possibilities for meaning-making and understanding. When schools limit themselves primarily to linguistic and mathematical forms, they effectively shut off access to other valuable ways of knowing and expressing knowledge.

The book emphasizes the importance of what Eisner calls "educational connoisseurship" and "educational criticism." Drawing from his background in art education, he applies concepts from aesthetic theory to educational practice. Educational connoisseurship involves the ability to perceive and appreciate the subtle qualities and nuances of educational experiences, while educational criticism involves the articulation and evaluation of these perceptions. This approach suggests that teaching and learning are fundamentally aesthetic activities that require sensitivity, judgment, and interpretive skill.

Eisner also explores the role of curriculum as a "mind-altering device," arguing that what students study literally shapes how they think and what they are able to perceive and understand. The curriculum is not merely a neutral vehicle for transmitting information but an active force in cognitive development. Different subjects and different approaches to those subjects cultivate different mental capacities and ways of processing information. This insight has profound implications for curriculum design and educational policy.

The book addresses the tension between standardization and diversity in education. While acknowledging the practical need for some common standards and assessments, Eisner warns against the homogenizing effects of excessive standardization. He advocates for educational approaches that honor individual differences in learning styles, interests, and talents, arguing that diversity in educational outcomes should be celebrated rather than eliminated.

Throughout the work, Eisner demonstrates how the arts serve as a crucial component of a well-rounded education. He argues that artistic activities develop forms of thinking that are essential for innovation, creativity, and deep understanding across all domains of knowledge. The arts teach students to attend to qualitative relationships, to think metaphorically, to tolerate ambiguity, and to appreciate multiple perspectives—all crucial skills for navigating an increasingly complex world.

The significance of this book lies in its challenge to narrow, mechanistic views of education and learning. At a time when educational policy increasingly emphasizes standardized testing and accountability measures, Eisner's work provides a compelling counterargument for educational approaches that honor the full spectrum of human intelligence and creativity. His ideas have influenced generations of educators, curriculum theorists, and policymakers who seek to create more humane and comprehensive educational experiences.

The book's enduring relevance is evident in contemporary discussions about multiple intelligences, differentiated instruction, and the importance of creativity in education. Eisner's vision of education as fundamentally concerned with expanding human consciousness and capability continues to inspire educators who believe that schools should cultivate not just academic achievement but also wisdom, sensitivity, and the capacity for meaningful engagement with the world.

## Theme Analysis

### Theme 1: Multiple Ways of Knowing
**Explanation:** Eisner argues that human beings possess various cognitive modes for understanding reality, extending beyond traditional academic subjects to include artistic, intuitive, and experiential forms of knowledge. This challenges educational systems that privilege only scientific and logical-mathematical thinking.
**Examples:** The book contrasts how a scientist, artist, and poet might each understand and represent the same natural phenomenon, demonstrating that each way of knowing reveals different aspects of reality and contributes unique insights.

### Theme 2: Forms of Representation
**Explanation:** Different symbolic systems—visual, auditory, kinesthetic, linguistic, and mathematical—offer distinct possibilities for encoding and communicating knowledge. Each form shapes both what can be known and how it can be expressed and shared.
**Examples:** Eisner illustrates how musical notation captures temporal and tonal relationships that written language cannot adequately convey, while visual art can express emotional and spatial concepts that resist verbal description.

### Theme 3: Educational Connoisseurship and Criticism
**Explanation:** Drawing from aesthetic theory, Eisner proposes that educational practice requires the same kind of refined perception and critical judgment that art critics bring to their work. This involves developing sensitivity to the qualitative dimensions of teaching and learning.
**Examples:** The book describes how skilled teachers, like art connoisseurs, learn to perceive subtle cues in classroom dynamics, student engagement, and learning processes that less experienced educators might miss.

### Theme 4: Curriculum as Mind-Altering Device
**Explanation:** The curriculum actively shapes cognitive development by determining which mental capacities are cultivated and which ways of thinking are emphasized. What students study literally changes how their minds work and what they are capable of perceiving.
**Examples:** Eisner demonstrates how studying mathematics develops logical reasoning and pattern recognition, while engagement with the arts cultivates metaphorical thinking and appreciation for qualitative relationships.

### Theme 5: Critique of Educational Standardization
**Explanation:** While acknowledging practical needs for common standards, Eisner warns against excessive standardization that homogenizes educational experiences and outcomes. He advocates for approaches that honor individual differences and diverse forms of excellence.
**Examples:** The book critiques assessment practices that reduce complex learning to standardized test scores, arguing that such measures fail to capture many important educational outcomes and may actually impede meaningful learning.

### Theme 6: The Arts as Essential Knowledge
**Explanation:** Artistic activities are not merely decorative additions to education but essential components that develop crucial cognitive capacities including creativity, metaphorical thinking, and the ability to work with ambiguity and multiple perspectives.
**Examples:** Eisner shows how engagement with visual arts teaches students to attend to relationships between form and content, while music education develops temporal reasoning and collaborative skills that transfer to other domains.
