# Results Based Leadership
**Author:** Ulrich

**Tags:** Management, OD HR

## Summary

"Results-Based Leadership" by Dave Ulrich, Jack Zenger, and Norm Smallwood presents a revolutionary approach to leadership that fundamentally challenges traditional leadership thinking. The authors argue that effective leadership cannot be measured solely by personal attributes, charisma, or management skills, but must be evaluated based on tangible business results and organizational outcomes.

The book's central premise is built around the Results-Based Leadership equation: Leadership = Attributes × Results. This formula suggests that leadership effectiveness is determined not just by what leaders know or how they behave (attributes), but by multiplying those attributes with the actual results they achieve. The authors contend that many leadership development programs focus exclusively on building leadership attributes—such as vision, communication skills, or emotional intelligence—while neglecting the critical component of delivering measurable business outcomes.

Ulrich, Zenger, and Smallwood identify four key domains where leaders must demonstrate results: employee results, organization results, customer results, and investor results. Employee results focus on building talent, engagement, and organizational capability. Organization results emphasize creating effective systems, processes, and culture. Customer results center on delivering value and building lasting relationships with clients. Investor results involve generating financial returns and long-term shareholder value. The authors argue that truly effective leaders must excel across all four domains rather than focusing on just one area.

The book introduces several practical frameworks for developing results-based leaders. The Leadership Brand concept suggests that organizations should develop leaders who embody and deliver on the company's brand promise to customers. This approach ensures that leadership development is aligned with business strategy and market positioning. The authors also present the Leadership Code, which identifies five fundamental leadership rules that transcend industry, geography, and organizational level.

A significant portion of the book addresses how organizations can shift from attribute-based to results-based leadership development. The authors provide specific methodologies for measuring leadership effectiveness, including balanced scorecards for leaders, 360-degree feedback systems that incorporate business results, and leadership assessment tools that evaluate both competencies and outcomes. They emphasize that leadership development investments should be treated like any other business investment, with clear metrics and expected returns.

The authors also explore the concept of leadership sustainability, arguing that results-based leaders create lasting organizational capabilities rather than short-term wins. They distinguish between leaders who achieve results through personal heroics versus those who build systems and develop others to sustain performance over time. The book emphasizes that the most effective leaders are those who can deliver results while simultaneously building the organizational capacity for future success.

Throughout the work, Ulrich, Zenger, and Smallwood provide numerous case studies and examples from various industries to illustrate their concepts. They demonstrate how companies like General Electric, Johnson & Johnson, and other leading organizations have successfully implemented results-based leadership approaches. These examples show how linking leadership development to business outcomes can drive significant improvements in organizational performance.

The book's significance lies in its practical approach to leadership development and its challenge to conventional wisdom. By insisting that leadership must be measured by results, the authors provide a framework that helps organizations make better decisions about leadership selection, development, and succession planning. The work has influenced countless organizations to reconsider their approach to leadership development and has contributed to a more business-focused perspective on leadership effectiveness.

"Results-Based Leadership" ultimately argues that the leadership field has been too focused on the "soft" aspects of leadership while neglecting the "hard" business outcomes that truly matter. The authors provide both the conceptual framework and practical tools necessary for organizations to develop leaders who can deliver sustainable business results while building organizational capability for the future.

## Theme Analysis

### Theme 1: The Leadership Equation
**Explanation:** The core concept that Leadership = Attributes × Results fundamentally redefines how leadership effectiveness should be measured. This equation emphasizes that attributes alone are insufficient without corresponding business outcomes.
**Examples:** The book contrasts leaders with strong attributes but poor results against those who excel in both dimensions, showing how the multiplication effect creates exponentially better leadership impact.

### Theme 2: Four Domains of Results
**Explanation:** Leaders must deliver results across employee, organization, customer, and investor domains to be truly effective. This comprehensive approach ensures balanced leadership that serves all stakeholder groups.
**Examples:** The authors provide assessment tools and case studies showing how leaders can measure and improve performance in each domain, with specific metrics and benchmarks for success.

### Theme 3: Leadership Brand Alignment
**Explanation:** Effective leaders embody and deliver on their organization's brand promise to customers. This creates consistency between internal leadership capabilities and external market positioning.
**Examples:** The book demonstrates how companies like Southwest Airlines and Disney develop leaders whose behaviors and decisions reinforce the company's brand identity and customer experience.

### Theme 4: Sustainable Results Creation
**Explanation:** Results-based leaders focus on building organizational capabilities and systems rather than relying on personal heroics. This approach ensures long-term performance beyond individual tenure.
**Examples:** Case studies illustrate the difference between leaders who achieve short-term wins through personal effort versus those who create lasting organizational improvements and develop successor capabilities.

### Theme 5: Measurement and Accountability
**Explanation:** Leadership development must be treated as a business investment with clear metrics, expected returns, and accountability mechanisms. This shifts leadership development from cost center to value creator.
**Examples:** The authors provide specific tools like leadership scorecards, ROI calculations for leadership development programs, and methods for tracking leadership impact on business outcomes.

### Theme 6: Integrated Leadership Development
**Explanation:** Effective leadership development programs must integrate attribute building with results delivery, creating learning experiences that develop both competencies and business acumen simultaneously.
**Examples:** The book showcases organizations that have redesigned their leadership programs to include real business challenges, measurable outcomes, and direct links to strategic objectives.
