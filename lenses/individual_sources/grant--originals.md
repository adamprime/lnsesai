# Originals
**Author:** Grant

**Tags:** Management

## Summary

"Originals: How Non-Conformists Move the World" by Adam Grant challenges conventional wisdom about innovation and creativity, exploring how individuals can champion new ideas and drive meaningful change. Grant, a renowned organizational psychologist, argues that originality isn't reserved for a select few but is a skill that can be developed and nurtured by anyone willing to challenge the status quo.

The book's central premise is that originals—people who dream up new ideas and take action to put them into the world—are not born but made. Grant dismantles common myths about creative individuals, revealing that many successful innovators are actually risk-averse, procrastinate strategically, and maintain day jobs while pursuing their passions. This counterintuitive finding suggests that the most groundbreaking ideas often come from those who appear conventional on the surface.

One of Grant's most significant insights involves the relationship between procrastination and creativity. Rather than being a productivity killer, moderate procrastination can actually enhance creative thinking by allowing ideas to incubate and evolve. This "pre-crastination" versus procrastination dynamic shows that rushing to execute ideas immediately may result in less innovative solutions than taking time to let concepts develop naturally.

The book extensively covers defensive pessimism as a valuable tool for originals. While optimism is often celebrated, Grant demonstrates how anticipating potential failures and obstacles can lead to better preparation and ultimately greater success. This approach allows innovators to identify weaknesses in their ideas and develop contingency plans, making their ventures more resilient.

Grant also addresses the critical challenge of speaking up and championing new ideas within organizations. He provides practical strategies for presenting novel concepts in ways that minimize resistance and maximize acceptance. This includes timing presentations appropriately, framing ideas in familiar terms, and building coalitions of support before formal proposals.

The author explores how originals can overcome the fear of failure that often paralyzes potential innovators. By reframing failure as learning opportunities and understanding that even successful originals have numerous failed attempts, individuals can develop the resilience necessary to persist through setbacks. Grant emphasizes that the key is not avoiding failure but learning to fail fast and iterate quickly.

Leadership plays a crucial role in fostering originality within organizations. Grant outlines how leaders can create environments that encourage dissent, reward creative thinking, and protect those who challenge existing practices. This includes establishing devil's advocate roles, encouraging diverse perspectives, and creating psychological safety for employees to voice unconventional ideas.

The book also delves into the importance of timing in bringing original ideas to market. Grant discusses how being a "first-mover" isn't always advantageous, and that "fast followers" who improve upon initial innovations often achieve greater success. This insight helps originals understand when to pioneer new territories and when to refine existing concepts.

Throughout the work, Grant provides extensive research-backed evidence and real-world examples from various fields, including business, politics, and social movements. He examines case studies ranging from entrepreneurs and artists to civil rights activists, demonstrating how originality manifests across different domains and contexts.

The significance of "Originals" lies in its practical approach to fostering innovation and change. Unlike books that simply celebrate creativity, Grant provides actionable strategies for developing original thinking and implementing new ideas effectively. The book is particularly valuable for managers, entrepreneurs, and anyone seeking to drive positive change in their organizations or communities.

Grant's work is especially relevant in today's rapidly changing business environment, where innovation is essential for survival and growth. By democratizing originality and showing that it's a learnable skill rather than an innate talent, the book empowers readers to become agents of change in their own right. The research-based approach lends credibility to the recommendations, making it a trusted resource for both individual development and organizational transformation.

## Theme Analysis

### Theme 1: Strategic Procrastination and Creative Incubation
**Explanation:** Grant reveals that moderate procrastination can actually enhance creativity by allowing ideas to develop and evolve naturally. This challenges the conventional wisdom that immediate action is always best, showing how strategic delays can lead to more innovative solutions.
**Examples:** The book discusses how Leonardo da Vinci's procrastination on the Mona Lisa led to artistic breakthroughs, and how entrepreneurs who delay launching their ventures often create more successful businesses than those who rush to market.

### Theme 2: Defensive Pessimism as a Success Strategy
**Explanation:** Rather than promoting blind optimism, Grant demonstrates how anticipating potential failures and obstacles can lead to better preparation and outcomes. This defensive approach helps originals identify weaknesses and develop robust contingency plans.
**Examples:** The book examines how successful entrepreneurs often envision worst-case scenarios to strengthen their business plans, and how activists prepare for opposition to make their movements more resilient.

### Theme 3: Risk Management and Portfolio Approaches
**Explanation:** Contrary to popular belief, many successful originals are actually risk-averse individuals who maintain safety nets while pursuing innovative ideas. They manage risk through diversification rather than putting everything on the line.
**Examples:** Grant highlights how many entrepreneurs keep their day jobs while building their startups, and how creative individuals often work on multiple projects simultaneously to spread risk and increase chances of success.

### Theme 4: Organizational Dissent and Speaking Truth to Power
**Explanation:** The book emphasizes the importance of creating environments where individuals feel safe to challenge existing practices and propose new ideas. This requires both courage from individuals and supportive leadership structures.
**Examples:** Grant discusses how companies like Bridgewater Associates institutionalize dissent through radical transparency, and how historical figures effectively challenged authority by building coalitions and timing their interventions strategically.

### Theme 5: Timing and Market Entry Strategies
**Explanation:** Grant challenges the first-mover advantage myth, showing that being too early or too late with innovations can be detrimental. Success often comes from understanding market readiness and entering at optimal moments.
**Examples:** The book analyzes how companies like Google succeeded by improving upon existing search engines rather than being first to market, and how social movements gain traction by launching when conditions are most favorable for change.

### Theme 6: Failure Reframing and Resilience Building
**Explanation:** The book teaches readers to view failure as an essential component of the innovation process rather than something to be avoided. This mindset shift enables greater experimentation and learning from setbacks.
**Examples:** Grant examines how successful originals maintain high failure rates but learn quickly from mistakes, and how organizations can create cultures that celebrate intelligent failures while learning from them systematically.
