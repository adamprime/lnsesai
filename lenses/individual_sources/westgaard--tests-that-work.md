# Tests That Work
**Author:** Westgaard

**Tags:** Thinking

## Summary

"Tests That Work: Designing and Delivering Fair and Practical Measurement Tools in the Workplace" by Odin Westgaard is a comprehensive guide that addresses one of the most critical yet often mishandled aspects of organizational development: workplace assessment and measurement. Published as both a practical handbook and theoretical framework, the book tackles the complex challenge of creating evaluation tools that are simultaneously fair, accurate, and practically implementable in real workplace environments.

Westgaard's central argument revolves around the premise that most workplace testing and measurement tools fail not because of poor intentions, but due to fundamental design flaws and implementation oversights. The author contends that effective workplace measurement requires a systematic approach that balances psychometric rigor with practical constraints, ensuring that tests serve their intended purpose while maintaining fairness and legal compliance.

The book's framework emphasizes the importance of understanding the specific context in which measurements will be used. Westgaard argues that workplace assessments must be designed with clear objectives, appropriate methodologies, and realistic expectations about what can and cannot be measured effectively. He stresses that successful measurement tools must align with organizational goals while respecting individual rights and maintaining professional standards.

A key strength of Westgaard's approach is his emphasis on the entire lifecycle of workplace measurement, from initial needs assessment through implementation and ongoing evaluation. He provides detailed guidance on identifying what should be measured, selecting appropriate measurement methods, and ensuring that the resulting data can be meaningfully interpreted and acted upon. The author particularly emphasizes the importance of validity and reliability in workplace contexts, where the stakes of measurement decisions can significantly impact both individual careers and organizational effectiveness.

The book addresses common pitfalls in workplace measurement, including the tendency to over-rely on standardized tests that may not be appropriate for specific workplace contexts, the failure to consider cultural and individual differences that can affect test performance, and the lack of proper validation procedures. Westgaard provides practical solutions for these challenges, offering step-by-step guidance for developing custom measurement tools when existing options are inadequate.

Westgaard also dedicates significant attention to the legal and ethical dimensions of workplace measurement. He emphasizes the importance of ensuring that measurement tools comply with relevant employment laws and professional standards, while also addressing the ethical obligations that organizations have to use fair and unbiased assessment methods. This includes detailed discussion of how to identify and mitigate potential sources of bias in measurement tools.

The practical orientation of the book is evident in its extensive use of real-world examples and case studies. Westgaard draws from his extensive experience in organizational development to provide concrete illustrations of both successful and unsuccessful measurement initiatives. These examples help readers understand not just what to do, but also what to avoid and how to recover from common mistakes.

The book's significance extends beyond its immediate practical applications. Westgaard's work contributes to the broader conversation about evidence-based management and the role of measurement in organizational decision-making. By providing a framework for creating reliable and valid measurement tools, the book supports the development of more effective and equitable workplace practices.

Westgaard's approach is particularly valuable for human resources professionals, organizational development specialists, and managers who need to make personnel decisions based on assessment data. The book provides these practitioners with the tools they need to evaluate existing measurement instruments and develop new ones when necessary. It also helps them understand the limitations of measurement tools and how to communicate assessment results appropriately to stakeholders.

The enduring relevance of "Tests That Work" lies in its recognition that effective workplace measurement is both an art and a science. While the book provides rigorous methodological guidance, it also acknowledges the importance of professional judgment and contextual sensitivity in applying measurement principles. This balanced approach makes the book valuable for both newcomers to the field and experienced practitioners seeking to refine their approach to workplace assessment.

## Theme Analysis

### Theme 1: Systematic Design Methodology
**Explanation:** Westgaard emphasizes that effective workplace measurement tools require a systematic, methodical approach to design rather than ad-hoc or intuitive development. This theme underscores the importance of following established principles of test construction and validation.
**Examples:** The book provides step-by-step procedures for needs assessment, item development, pilot testing, and validation. Westgaard illustrates how systematic approaches prevent common errors like measuring irrelevant constructs or using inappropriate item formats.

### Theme 2: Contextual Appropriateness
**Explanation:** A central theme is that measurement tools must be specifically designed for their intended workplace context rather than simply adapted from other settings. Generic or borrowed assessments often fail to capture the unique requirements and constraints of specific organizational environments.
**Examples:** Westgaard demonstrates how job-specific competency assessments require different approaches than general aptitude tests, and how organizational culture influences the appropriateness of different measurement strategies.

### Theme 3: Fairness and Legal Compliance
**Explanation:** The book extensively addresses the legal and ethical obligations organizations have to ensure their measurement tools are fair, unbiased, and compliant with employment law. This theme emphasizes that effective measurement must balance organizational needs with individual rights.
**Examples:** Detailed discussions of how to identify and eliminate cultural bias in assessment items, ensure accessibility for individuals with disabilities, and document the job-relatedness of measurement tools for legal defensibility.

### Theme 4: Practical Implementation Challenges
**Explanation:** Westgaard recognizes that even well-designed measurement tools can fail if implementation is poorly planned or executed. This theme addresses the real-world constraints and challenges that affect how measurement tools function in practice.
**Examples:** The book covers issues like resource constraints, time limitations, resistance from test-takers or managers, and the need to balance measurement precision with practical efficiency in workplace settings.

### Theme 5: Validation and Continuous Improvement
**Explanation:** A recurring theme is that measurement tools require ongoing validation and refinement rather than one-time development. Westgaard emphasizes that effective measurement is an iterative process that must adapt to changing organizational needs and conditions.
**Examples:** The book provides frameworks for collecting validity evidence over time, monitoring measurement tool performance, and making systematic improvements based on empirical data and user feedback.

### Theme 6: Communication and Stakeholder Management
**Explanation:** Westgaard emphasizes that the success of workplace measurement initiatives depends heavily on effective communication with various stakeholders, including test-takers, managers, and organizational leaders. This theme addresses how to build support and understanding for measurement programs.
**Examples:** Guidance on explaining assessment results to non-technical audiences, managing expectations about what measurement tools can and cannot accomplish, and building organizational buy-in for measurement initiatives.
