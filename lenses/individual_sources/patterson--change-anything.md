# Change Anything
**Author:** Patterson

**Tags:** Managing Change

## Comprehensive Summary

**Change Anything: The New Science of Personal Success** by Kerry Patterson, Joseph Grenny, David Maxfield, Ron McMillan, and Al Switzler represents a groundbreaking approach to personal transformation based on decades of behavioral research. Published in 2011, this work emerged during a period of increasing interest in evidence-based approaches to behavior change, drawing from advances in psychology, neuroscience, and social science research.

**Overview and Key Takeaways**

The central premise of "Change Anything" is that traditional approaches to personal change fail because they rely too heavily on willpower and motivation alone. The authors argue that successful, lasting change requires a comprehensive strategy that addresses six sources of influence that shape human behavior. Rather than focusing solely on individual determination, the book presents a systematic methodology for creating an environment where change becomes inevitable.

The authors challenge the conventional wisdom that change is primarily about personal motivation and self-discipline. Instead, they demonstrate through extensive research that sustainable behavior change occurs when multiple forces align to support the desired transformation. This represents a paradigm shift from individual-focused change strategies to a more holistic, systems-based approach.

**Key Frameworks and Concepts**

The book's foundation rests on the Six Sources of Influence model, which categorizes the forces that drive human behavior into two dimensions: three levels of influence (Personal, Social, and Structural) crossed with two types of motivation (Motivation and Ability). This creates a comprehensive framework for understanding and manipulating the factors that determine whether change efforts succeed or fail.

At the Personal level, the authors distinguish between personal motivation (helping individuals want to change) and personal ability (helping them develop the skills to change). The Social dimension examines how other people influence behavior through social motivation (peer pressure and social approval) and social ability (help and support from others). The Structural level focuses on environmental factors, including structural motivation (rewards and consequences) and structural ability (environmental design that makes change easier or harder).

The concept of "crucial moments" plays a central role in the methodology. These are the specific times when individuals face the choice between old and new behaviors. The authors argue that by identifying and preparing for these predictable moments of temptation or challenge, individuals can dramatically increase their success rates.

Another key framework is the idea of "vital behaviors" – the small number of high-leverage actions that, when changed, create disproportionate results. Rather than attempting to change everything at once, the methodology focuses on identifying and mastering these critical behaviors that drive broader transformation.

**The Science Behind the Approach**

The book draws extensively from behavioral research, including studies of successful "changers" – individuals who have accomplished significant personal transformations. The authors studied people who had successfully overcome various challenges, from weight loss and addiction recovery to career transitions and relationship improvements. This research revealed common patterns in how successful changers approached their transformation efforts.

The work also incorporates insights from neuroscience about how the brain processes decisions and forms habits. The authors explain how emotional and rational thinking systems interact, and how understanding these processes can help individuals make better choices during crucial moments.

**Practical Application and Methodology**

The book provides a systematic process for applying the six sources of influence to any change challenge. This includes diagnostic tools for identifying which sources of influence are currently working for or against the desired change, and specific strategies for strengthening supportive influences while weakening opposing ones.

The methodology emphasizes the importance of measurement and tracking, providing frameworks for monitoring progress and adjusting strategies based on results. The authors argue that most change efforts fail because people don't systematically measure their progress or learn from their setbacks.

**Significance and Impact**

"Change Anything" has had substantial impact in both personal development and organizational change fields. The book's evidence-based approach has influenced how coaches, therapists, and change consultants approach their work with clients. Its systematic methodology provides a more scientific alternative to traditional self-help approaches that often rely on inspiration rather than proven strategies.

The work has been particularly influential in corporate training and development programs, where organizations have adopted the six sources framework for supporting employee behavior change initiatives. The book's emphasis on environmental design and social support has also influenced workplace wellness programs and safety initiatives.

The reception has been generally positive, with readers and reviewers praising the book's practical, research-based approach. Critics have noted that while the framework is comprehensive, it can be complex to implement fully, requiring significant commitment and systematic application.

The lasting influence of "Change Anything" lies in its contribution to making behavior change more scientific and systematic. By providing a comprehensive framework that addresses multiple influences on behavior, the book has helped move the field beyond simplistic approaches that rely primarily on motivation and willpower. This has implications not only for individual change efforts but also for how organizations, communities, and societies approach large-scale behavior change challenges.

## Detailed Theme Analysis

### Theme 1: The Six Sources of Influence Model
**Explanation:** This comprehensive framework categorizes all influences on human behavior into six distinct sources, created by crossing three levels (Personal, Social, Structural) with two types (Motivation and Ability). The model provides a systematic way to diagnose why change efforts fail and design interventions that address multiple influence sources simultaneously. Rather than relying on a single approach, successful change requires orchestrating multiple sources to work in harmony. This framework represents the book's core methodology and distinguishes it from traditional change approaches.
**Manifestation in Book:** The authors use this model to analyze various change scenarios, from weight loss to addiction recovery. They demonstrate how failed change attempts typically address only one or two sources, while successful changers systematically work on four or more sources. The book provides detailed strategies for strengthening each source, such as using personal motivation techniques like connecting change to deeply held values, or structural ability interventions like redesigning environments to make good choices easier.

### Theme 2: Crucial Moments and Decision Points
**Explanation:** The concept of crucial moments identifies the specific times when individuals face choices between old and new behaviors. These predictable moments of temptation or challenge are when change efforts typically succeed or fail. By identifying these moments in advance and developing specific strategies for navigating them, individuals can dramatically improve their success rates. The authors argue that most change efforts fail because people don't adequately prepare for these predictable challenges.
**Manifestation in Book:** The book provides detailed examples of how to identify crucial moments, such as the moment a dieter faces dessert or when someone trying to exercise encounters a busy schedule. The authors offer specific techniques for managing these moments, including mental rehearsal, environmental modifications, and social support strategies. They emphasize creating detailed "if-then" plans that specify exactly how to respond when crucial moments arise.

### Theme 3: Vital Behaviors Identification
**Explanation:** Vital behaviors are the small number of high-leverage actions that create disproportionate results in achieving change goals. Rather than attempting to change everything simultaneously, the methodology focuses on identifying and mastering these critical behaviors that drive broader transformation. This concept helps individuals avoid the common mistake of trying to change too many things at once, which often leads to failure and overwhelm.
**Manifestation in Book:** The authors provide frameworks for identifying vital behaviors through analysis of successful models and careful observation of what actions most directly influence desired outcomes. They use examples like focusing on specific eating behaviors rather than general "healthy eating," or identifying particular communication patterns that improve relationships rather than trying to change entire personality traits.

### Theme 4: Personal Motivation Through Values Connection
**Explanation:** This theme addresses how to create lasting motivation by connecting desired changes to deeply held personal values and identity. The authors argue that surface-level motivations (like appearance or external approval) are insufficient for sustaining long-term change. Instead, successful changers connect their goals to fundamental aspects of who they are and what they believe is important. This creates intrinsic motivation that persists through challenges and setbacks.
**Manifestation in Book:** The book provides exercises for identifying core values and connecting them to change goals. Examples include helping someone connect weight loss to their value of being a good parent who models healthy behavior, or linking career change to values of authenticity and contribution. The authors show how successful changers regularly remind themselves of these deeper connections, especially during difficult moments.

### Theme 5: Social Influence and Support Systems
**Explanation:** This theme encompasses both social motivation (how others influence our desire to change) and social ability (how others help us develop skills and provide support). The authors demonstrate that change rarely happens in isolation and that the people around us significantly influence our success or failure. This includes both formal support systems and informal social networks that either reinforce or undermine change efforts.
**Manifestation in Book:** The book provides strategies for building supportive social networks, including how to communicate change goals effectively, how to ask for specific types of support, and how to manage relationships with people who might sabotage change efforts. Examples include creating accountability partnerships, joining communities of people working on similar changes, and learning to navigate social situations that present challenges to new behaviors.

### Theme 6: Environmental Design and Structural Changes
**Explanation:** This theme focuses on how physical and organizational environments can be designed to make desired behaviors easier and undesired behaviors more difficult. The authors argue that willpower is a limited resource and that smart environmental design can reduce the need for constant self-control. By changing the context in which decisions are made, individuals can dramatically improve their success rates without relying solely on motivation and discipline.
**Manifestation in Book:** The book provides numerous examples of environmental modifications, such as removing tempting foods from easily accessible locations, setting up exercise equipment in visible places, or using technology to create automatic reminders and barriers. The authors show how successful changers systematically analyze their environments and make strategic modifications that support their goals.

### Theme 7: Measurement and Feedback Systems
**Explanation:** This theme emphasizes the critical importance of tracking progress and creating feedback loops that provide regular information about how change efforts are progressing. The authors argue that most change efforts fail because people don't systematically measure their progress or learn from their experiences. Effective measurement systems provide both motivation and course-correction information that keeps change efforts on track.
**Manifestation in Book:** The book provides frameworks for designing measurement systems that track both leading indicators (behaviors) and lagging indicators (results). Examples include daily behavior tracking, regular progress reviews, and systems for learning from both successes and setbacks. The authors show how successful changers use data to continuously refine their approaches and maintain motivation over time.

### Theme 8: The Failure of Willpower-Only Approaches
**Explanation:** Throughout the book, the authors systematically debunk the myth that successful change is primarily about willpower, motivation, and personal discipline. They present research showing that these individual factors, while important, are insufficient for creating lasting transformation. This theme serves as the foundation for why a multi-source approach is necessary and challenges readers to move beyond traditional self-help thinking.
**Manifestation in Book:** The authors use research studies and real-world examples to demonstrate how even highly motivated individuals fail when they rely solely on willpower. They show how successful changers differ not in their level of motivation but in their systematic approach to addressing multiple sources of influence. This theme appears throughout the book as the authors contrast their methodology with conventional approaches to personal change.
