# How Great Leaders Think
**Author:** Bolman, Deal

**Tags:** Leadership, Managing Change

## Summary

"How Great Leaders Think" by Lee G. Bolman and Terrence E. Deal presents a comprehensive framework for understanding leadership through multiple perspectives, building on their influential four-frame model first introduced in "Reframing Organizations." The authors argue that exceptional leaders distinguish themselves not through charisma or innate talent alone, but through their ability to think about situations from multiple angles and adapt their approach accordingly.

The book's central premise is that most leaders operate from a single, dominant perspective, which limits their effectiveness in complex organizational environments. Great leaders, however, develop the cognitive flexibility to view challenges through four distinct frames: structural, human resource, political, and symbolic. This multi-frame thinking enables them to diagnose problems more accurately, generate creative solutions, and respond appropriately to diverse stakeholder needs.

The **structural frame** focuses on goals, roles, policies, and hierarchies. Leaders operating from this perspective emphasize efficiency, clear expectations, and systematic approaches to problem-solving. They excel at creating order and ensuring accountability but may struggle with interpersonal dynamics or organizational culture issues.

The **human resource frame** centers on people's needs, skills, and relationships. These leaders prioritize employee development, participation, and empowerment. They understand that organizations succeed when individuals feel valued and engaged, though they may sometimes underestimate the importance of structure or political realities.

The **political frame** recognizes organizations as arenas of competing interests where resources are scarce and conflict is inevitable. Leaders using this lens focus on building coalitions, negotiating agreements, and managing power dynamics. They excel at navigating complex stakeholder relationships but may be perceived as manipulative if they rely too heavily on this approach.

The **symbolic frame** emphasizes meaning, culture, and inspiration. These leaders understand that people need purpose and that symbols, rituals, and stories shape organizational identity. They excel at creating vision and motivating others through shared values, though they may struggle with operational details.

Bolman and Deal argue that situational leadership requires matching the appropriate frame to specific circumstances. Crisis situations may demand structural clarity, while major changes might require symbolic leadership to inspire and unite people. Effective leaders develop the ability to reframe situations, shifting their perspective when initial approaches prove inadequate.

The authors emphasize that reframing is both an analytical tool and a leadership skill. It involves stepping back from immediate reactions, considering alternative interpretations, and choosing responses that address the real underlying issues. This process requires intellectual humility—acknowledging that one's initial perspective may be incomplete or incorrect.

The book provides numerous real-world examples and case studies demonstrating how leaders have successfully applied multi-frame thinking. These examples span various sectors, from business and education to government and non-profits, illustrating the universal applicability of the framework.

Beyond individual leadership development, the authors address how organizations can cultivate multi-frame thinking throughout their ranks. They discuss the importance of diverse leadership teams where different members bring strength in different frames, creating collective wisdom that surpasses any individual leader's capabilities.

The significance of this work lies in its practical approach to leadership development. Rather than promoting a one-size-fits-all leadership style, Bolman and Deal provide a flexible framework that leaders can adapt to their unique contexts. The book challenges readers to expand their thinking beyond their comfort zones and develop greater cognitive complexity.

This framework has proven particularly valuable in today's rapidly changing business environment, where leaders face unprecedented challenges requiring innovative solutions. The ability to reframe problems and view them from multiple perspectives has become essential for navigating complexity, managing diverse teams, and leading organizational transformation.

"How Great Leaders Think" ultimately argues that leadership excellence comes not from perfecting a single approach, but from developing the wisdom to know when and how to shift perspectives. This meta-cognitive skill—thinking about thinking—represents the highest level of leadership development and distinguishes truly great leaders from merely competent ones.

## Theme Analysis

### Theme 1: Multi-Frame Leadership Perspective
**Explanation:** The core theme emphasizes that great leaders don't rely on a single worldview but instead develop the ability to see situations through multiple lenses simultaneously. This cognitive flexibility allows them to understand complex organizational dynamics more completely and respond more effectively to diverse challenges.
**Examples:** The book illustrates how a CEO facing declining performance might use structural analysis to examine processes, human resource perspective to assess employee engagement, political lens to understand stakeholder conflicts, and symbolic frame to evaluate cultural issues.

### Theme 2: Situational Adaptability and Reframing
**Explanation:** Effective leadership requires matching the appropriate frame to specific circumstances and being willing to shift perspectives when initial approaches prove inadequate. This theme emphasizes that leadership is contextual and that great leaders develop sensitivity to situational demands.
**Examples:** The authors show how crisis situations often require structural leadership for clarity and direction, while organizational transformations may demand symbolic leadership to inspire and create meaning around change initiatives.

### Theme 3: The Four Frames as Diagnostic Tools
**Explanation:** Each frame—structural, human resource, political, and symbolic—serves as both a lens for understanding organizational dynamics and a toolkit for leadership action. The frames help leaders diagnose what's really happening beneath surface symptoms and choose appropriate interventions.
**Examples:** When facing resistance to change, leaders learn to examine whether the issue stems from unclear structures, unaddressed human concerns, political opposition from threatened stakeholders, or symbolic conflicts with organizational culture and values.

### Theme 4: Cognitive Complexity and Leadership Development
**Explanation:** The book argues that leadership development is fundamentally about increasing cognitive complexity—the ability to hold multiple perspectives simultaneously and think about one's own thinking processes. This meta-cognitive skill distinguishes exceptional leaders from average ones.
**Examples:** The authors demonstrate how leaders can develop this complexity through deliberate practice in reframing exercises, seeking diverse perspectives, and reflecting on their default assumptions and blind spots in various leadership situations.

### Theme 5: Organizational Culture and Symbolic Leadership
**Explanation:** The symbolic frame receives particular attention as leaders often underestimate the power of meaning, ritual, and culture in organizational life. Great leaders understand that people need purpose and that symbolic actions often carry more weight than rational arguments.
**Examples:** The book provides examples of leaders who successfully transformed organizations by paying attention to symbols, stories, and ceremonies, showing how symbolic leadership can inspire commitment and create shared identity during times of change.

### Theme 6: Collaborative Leadership and Team Diversity
**Explanation:** Rather than expecting individual leaders to master all frames equally, the authors advocate for building leadership teams where different members bring complementary frame strengths. This collective approach leverages diverse thinking styles for better organizational outcomes.
**Examples:** The book illustrates how effective leadership teams might include members who naturally excel in different frames—combining analytical thinkers, people-oriented leaders, politically savvy negotiators, and visionary inspirers to create comprehensive leadership capacity.
