# Punished By Rewards
**Author:** Kohn

**Tags:** Motivation

## Summary

"Punished by Rewards: The Trouble with Gold Stars, Incentive Plans, A's, Praise, and Other Bribes" by Alfie Kohn presents a revolutionary challenge to one of the most fundamental assumptions in modern society: that rewards motivate people to perform better. Through extensive research and compelling logic, Kohn argues that external rewards and punishments not only fail to produce lasting positive change but actually undermine the very behaviors and values they're intended to promote.

The book's central thesis is that behaviorist approaches to motivation—whether in schools, workplaces, or homes—are fundamentally flawed. Kohn demonstrates that when people are offered rewards for performing tasks, their intrinsic motivation decreases, their creativity suffers, and their long-term engagement with the activity diminishes. This phenomenon occurs because rewards shift focus from the inherent satisfaction of an activity to the external payoff, transforming what might have been meaningful work into mere compliance.

Kohn systematically dismantles the effectiveness of pay-for-performance plans in corporate settings, showing that the more organizations rely on incentives, the worse outcomes become. He presents evidence that such systems create competition rather than collaboration, encourage short-term thinking over long-term innovation, and ultimately reduce both productivity and job satisfaction. Workers become focused on gaming the system to earn rewards rather than genuinely improving their performance or contributing to organizational goals.

In educational contexts, Kohn argues that grades, gold stars, and other academic rewards similarly corrupt the learning process. Students become grade-focused rather than learning-focused, choosing easier tasks to ensure success rather than challenging themselves to grow. The book advocates for helping students "forget that grades exist" by creating environments where learning is valued for its own sake. This doesn't mean lowering standards, but rather shifting from external evaluation to internal motivation and genuine understanding.

Perhaps most controversially, Kohn extends his critique to praise itself, arguing that even positive verbal feedback can function as a "verbal bribe" that makes children dependent on adult approval rather than developing their own sense of satisfaction and self-evaluation. He distinguishes between praise that manipulates behavior and genuine encouragement that supports autonomy and growth.

The book introduces the concept of the "three C's" that rewards undermine: Collaboration (rewards promote competition), Creativity (rewards encourage safe, conventional approaches), and intrinsic motivation (Curiosity). Kohn explains how reward systems create a controlling environment that stifles these essential human capacities.

Kohn doesn't merely criticize existing systems; he offers alternative approaches based on supporting autonomy, fostering intrinsic motivation, and creating environments where people can experience genuine satisfaction from their work and learning. He advocates for collaborative rather than competitive structures, meaningful rather than arbitrary tasks, and supportive rather than controlling relationships.

The significance of "Punished by Rewards" extends far beyond academic theory. It challenges parents, teachers, and managers to reconsider fundamental assumptions about human motivation and to develop more effective, humane approaches to encouraging positive behavior and performance. The book has influenced educational reform movements, progressive parenting approaches, and organizational development practices worldwide.

Kohn's work is particularly relevant in an era of increasing emphasis on standardized testing, performance metrics, and incentive-based systems. His research-backed arguments provide a compelling case for moving beyond behaviorist approaches toward methods that honor human dignity and support genuine growth and development.

## Theme Analysis

### Theme 1: The Failure of Behaviorism
**Explanation:** Kohn systematically debunks behaviorist psychology's core assumption that rewards increase desired behaviors. He demonstrates that external motivators actually decrease intrinsic motivation and long-term performance.
**Examples:** Corporate incentive programs that reduce productivity, classroom reward systems that diminish love of learning, and parenting approaches that create compliance rather than genuine cooperation.

### Theme 2: Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic Motivation
**Explanation:** The book explores the crucial distinction between internal satisfaction derived from meaningful activity and external rewards imposed by others. Kohn argues that intrinsic motivation is more powerful and sustainable than extrinsic rewards.
**Examples:** Students who lose interest in reading when given rewards for books read, employees who become less creative when offered bonuses for innovation, and children who stop helping when previously rewarded for good behavior.

### Theme 3: The Corruption of Relationships
**Explanation:** Rewards fundamentally alter relationships by introducing elements of control and manipulation. They transform collaborative partnerships into power dynamics where one party controls the other through incentives.
**Examples:** Teacher-student relationships damaged by grading systems, parent-child bonds strained by reward charts, and workplace relationships undermined by competitive incentive structures.

### Theme 4: Alternatives to Control
**Explanation:** Rather than simply criticizing reward systems, Kohn presents alternative approaches based on collaboration, autonomy support, and intrinsic motivation. These methods respect human dignity while achieving better outcomes.
**Examples:** Collaborative learning environments, workplace democracy initiatives, and parenting approaches that involve children in problem-solving rather than imposing external consequences.

### Theme 5: The Three C's: Collaboration, Creativity, and Curiosity
**Explanation:** Kohn identifies three essential human capacities that reward systems systematically undermine. These represent what we lose when we rely on external motivators rather than supporting natural human tendencies.
**Examples:** Competitive grading that prevents students from helping each other learn, incentive systems that discourage risk-taking and innovation, and reward programs that make exploration contingent on external approval.

### Theme 6: Systemic Change vs. Individual Solutions
**Explanation:** The book argues that the problem with rewards is structural rather than individual, requiring systemic changes to institutions rather than simply better implementation of reward systems.
**Examples:** Educational reform that eliminates grades rather than improving them, organizational restructuring that removes hierarchical incentives, and parenting approaches that focus on family cooperation rather than individual behavior modification.
