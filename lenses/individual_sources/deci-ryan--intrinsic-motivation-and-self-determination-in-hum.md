# Intrinsic Motivation and Self Determination in Human Behaviour
**Author:** Deci, Ryan

**Tags:** Motivation

## Summary

"Intrinsic Motivation and Self-Determination in Human Behavior" by Edward Deci and Richard Ryan presents a groundbreaking psychological theory that fundamentally challenges traditional behaviorist approaches to understanding human motivation. The book introduces Self-Determination Theory (SDT), which argues that humans possess innate psychological needs that, when satisfied, promote optimal functioning, well-being, and intrinsic motivation.

The central thesis revolves around the distinction between intrinsic and extrinsic motivation. Intrinsic motivation refers to engaging in activities for their inherent satisfaction and enjoyment, while extrinsic motivation involves performing behaviors to achieve separable outcomes like rewards, approval, or to avoid punishment. The authors demonstrate through extensive research that intrinsic motivation leads to greater creativity, enhanced performance, improved learning, and increased psychological well-being compared to extrinsic motivators.

At the heart of SDT are three basic psychological needs: autonomy, competence, and relatedness. Autonomy refers to the need to feel volitional and self-directed in one's actions rather than controlled by external forces. Competence involves the need to feel effective and capable of achieving desired outcomes. Relatedness encompasses the need to feel connected to others and experience a sense of belonging. The authors argue that these needs are universal across cultures and developmental stages, and their satisfaction is essential for psychological growth and well-being.

The book presents a comprehensive framework called the Cognitive Evaluation Theory (CET), which explains how external events affect intrinsic motivation. CET demonstrates that rewards, deadlines, surveillance, and other controlling factors can undermine intrinsic motivation by diminishing one's sense of autonomy and self-determination. Conversely, environments that support choice, provide optimal challenges, and offer positive feedback enhance intrinsic motivation by satisfying the basic psychological needs.

Deci and Ryan also introduce the concept of internalization and integration, explaining how individuals can transform externally regulated behaviors into more autonomous forms of motivation. They describe a continuum of motivation ranging from amotivation through various forms of extrinsic motivation to intrinsic motivation. This continuum includes external regulation (behavior controlled by rewards and punishments), introjected regulation (behavior driven by internal pressure and guilt), identified regulation (behavior aligned with personal goals), integrated regulation (behavior consistent with one's values and identity), and finally intrinsic motivation.

The authors provide substantial empirical evidence supporting their theory through laboratory experiments, field studies, and cross-cultural research. They examine applications across various domains including education, work, parenting, healthcare, and psychotherapy. In educational settings, they show how autonomy-supportive teaching methods lead to better learning outcomes and increased student engagement compared to controlling approaches. In workplace contexts, they demonstrate that jobs designed to satisfy basic psychological needs result in higher job satisfaction, better performance, and reduced turnover.

The book also addresses the role of social contexts in supporting or thwarting psychological needs. The authors emphasize that environments characterized by autonomy support, structure, and involvement are most conducive to intrinsic motivation and well-being. They provide practical guidelines for creating such environments in various settings, making their theoretical insights applicable to real-world situations.

One of the most significant contributions of this work is its challenge to the widespread use of external rewards and punishments as motivational tools. The authors present compelling evidence that while rewards may produce short-term compliance, they often undermine long-term motivation and creativity. This has profound implications for educational practices, management strategies, and parenting approaches.

The book's significance extends beyond psychology into fields such as education, organizational behavior, healthcare, and public policy. It provides a scientific foundation for understanding human motivation that emphasizes the importance of supporting people's inherent growth tendencies rather than trying to control their behavior through external means. The theory has influenced countless researchers and practitioners, leading to more humane and effective approaches to motivation in various contexts.

SDT represents a paradigm shift from viewing humans as passive responders to external stimuli to understanding them as active organisms with inherent tendencies toward growth, integration, and well-being. This perspective has profound implications for how we structure societies, organizations, and relationships to support human flourishing.

## Theme Analysis

### Theme 1: The Distinction Between Intrinsic and Extrinsic Motivation
**Explanation:** This fundamental distinction forms the cornerstone of SDT, arguing that the source and quality of motivation significantly impact behavior, performance, and well-being. Intrinsic motivation leads to more sustainable engagement and better outcomes than extrinsic motivation.
**Examples:** The book presents research showing that students who are intrinsically motivated to learn demonstrate better conceptual understanding and creativity compared to those motivated by grades or external rewards. Similarly, employees who find their work inherently satisfying show greater innovation and job satisfaction.

### Theme 2: Basic Psychological Needs Theory
**Explanation:** The three universal needs of autonomy, competence, and relatedness serve as the foundation for human motivation and well-being. When these needs are satisfied, people experience optimal functioning; when thwarted, they experience diminished motivation and psychological distress.
**Examples:** The authors demonstrate how classroom environments that provide choice (autonomy), appropriate challenges (competence), and caring relationships (relatedness) foster student engagement and learning. Conversely, controlling environments that micromanage behavior undermine these needs and reduce motivation.

### Theme 3: The Undermining Effect of External Rewards
**Explanation:** One of the most controversial aspects of SDT is the demonstration that external rewards can actually decrease intrinsic motivation under certain conditions. This challenges conventional wisdom about the effectiveness of reward systems.
**Examples:** Laboratory studies show that children who receive rewards for activities they initially found interesting subsequently show less interest in those activities when rewards are removed. The book also examines how performance-based pay systems can sometimes reduce creativity and job satisfaction in the workplace.

### Theme 4: Internalization and Integration Processes
**Explanation:** The book explains how individuals can transform externally motivated behaviors into more autonomous forms through processes of internalization and integration. This provides a pathway for developing self-determined motivation even for initially uninteresting activities.
**Examples:** The authors describe how students can move from doing homework merely to avoid punishment to eventually valuing education as important for their personal goals. They also examine how individuals in therapy can internalize healthy behaviors by connecting them to their core values and identity.

### Theme 5: Social Context and Environmental Support
**Explanation:** The social environment plays a crucial role in either supporting or undermining basic psychological needs. Autonomy-supportive contexts foster intrinsic motivation, while controlling contexts diminish it.
**Examples:** The book contrasts autonomy-supportive teaching styles (providing rationales, offering choices, acknowledging feelings) with controlling styles (using pressure, deadlines, and surveillance) and shows their differential effects on student motivation and learning. Similar comparisons are made between different management and parenting styles.

### Theme 6: Applications Across Life Domains
**Explanation:** SDT's principles apply across various life contexts, from education and work to healthcare and relationships. This universality demonstrates the broad relevance of the theory for understanding human behavior.
**Examples:** The authors examine how SDT applies to medical adherence (patients are more likely to follow treatment regimens when they feel autonomous in their healthcare decisions), exercise motivation (intrinsic motivation leads to more sustained physical activity), and psychotherapy (supporting client autonomy leads to better therapeutic outcomes).
