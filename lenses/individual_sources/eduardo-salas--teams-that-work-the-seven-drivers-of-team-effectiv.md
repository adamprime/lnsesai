# Teams That Work: The Seven Drivers of Team Effectiveness
**Author:** Eduardo Salas

**Tags:** Teaming

## Comprehensive Summary

**Teams That Work: The Seven Drivers of Team Effectiveness** by Scott I. Tannenbaum and Eduardo Salas represents a seminal contribution to organizational psychology and team dynamics literature. Published by Oxford University Press in 2021, this 257-page work addresses one of the most pressing challenges in modern organizational life: why some teams excel while others struggle despite having similar resources and talent.

**Overview and Key Takeaways**

The book's central premise revolves around a fundamental question that has plagued organizations across industries: what distinguishes high-performing teams from those that underperform? Tannenbaum and Salas, drawing from decades of research in team science and organizational psychology, argue that team effectiveness is not a matter of chance or personality compatibility, but rather the result of seven specific, measurable drivers that can be developed and optimized.

The authors challenge the conventional wisdom that team success is primarily about assembling the right mix of personalities or relying on natural chemistry. Instead, they present a evidence-based framework demonstrating that team effectiveness emerges from deliberate attention to specific behavioral and structural elements. This approach transforms team development from an art form based on intuition into a science grounded in empirical research.

The seven drivers identified by the authors represent both foundational elements and dynamic processes that successful teams consistently demonstrate. These drivers are not independent variables but interconnected elements that reinforce each other when properly implemented. The book emphasizes that understanding these drivers is crucial for team leaders, team members, senior executives, and organizational consultants who seek to create and sustain high-performing collaborative environments.

**Setting and Context**

The book emerges from the contemporary workplace context where collaboration has become the dominant mode of organizational work. The authors situate their analysis within the reality of modern organizations where traditional hierarchical structures have given way to matrix organizations, cross-functional teams, virtual collaboration, and project-based work arrangements. This shift has made team effectiveness not just desirable but essential for organizational survival and competitive advantage.

The temporal context is particularly significant as the book addresses challenges that have intensified in the 21st century workplace: remote work, diverse and distributed teams, rapid technological change, and increased complexity of organizational challenges. The authors acknowledge that while teamwork has always been important, the stakes have never been higher, and the traditional approaches to team building have proven inadequate for contemporary challenges.

The social context includes the recognition that employees today expect meaningful collaboration and that managers are increasingly evaluated on their ability to lead effective teams. The book addresses the frustration and inefficiency that results from poorly functioning teams, which the authors argue is not just a productivity issue but also affects employee engagement, retention, and organizational culture.

**Key Frameworks and Methodological Approach**

The authors employ a systematic, research-based approach that distinguishes their work from popular management literature. Their methodology combines meta-analysis of existing team research, longitudinal studies of team performance, and practical case studies from various organizational contexts. This triangulated approach provides both theoretical rigor and practical applicability.

The central framework revolves around the seven drivers, which the authors present as both diagnostic tools and developmental targets. Each driver is supported by specific behavioral indicators, measurement criteria, and improvement strategies. The framework is designed to be actionable, allowing teams and organizations to assess their current state and identify specific areas for development.

The authors also introduce the concept of "team effectiveness" as a multidimensional construct that includes not only performance outcomes but also team member satisfaction, learning, and adaptability. This expanded definition recognizes that sustainable team success requires attention to both task accomplishment and team maintenance functions.

Another key methodological element is the authors' emphasis on evidence-based practice. Throughout the book, they distinguish between popular team-building activities that lack empirical support and interventions that have been validated through rigorous research. This approach helps readers avoid common pitfalls and focus their efforts on strategies with proven effectiveness.

The framework also incorporates situational factors, acknowledging that team effectiveness strategies must be adapted to specific contexts, including organizational culture, task complexity, team composition, and environmental constraints. This contingency approach prevents the oversimplification that characterizes many team development approaches.

**Significance and Impact**

The significance of "Teams That Work" lies in its synthesis of decades of team science research into a practical, actionable framework. The book bridges the gap between academic research and organizational practice, making sophisticated team science accessible to practitioners while maintaining scientific rigor. This contribution is particularly valuable given the proliferation of team-building approaches that lack empirical foundation.

The book's impact extends beyond individual team development to organizational design and leadership development. By providing clear criteria for team effectiveness, it enables organizations to make more informed decisions about team composition, structure, and support systems. The framework also provides a common language for discussing team performance, facilitating more productive conversations about team development across organizational levels.

The work has been particularly influential in healthcare, where team effectiveness can have life-or-death implications, as well as in technology companies, consulting firms, and other knowledge-intensive industries where collaboration is central to value creation. The book's emphasis on measurable outcomes and evidence-based interventions aligns with the growing demand for accountability in organizational development initiatives.

The timing of the book's publication, coinciding with the global shift to remote and hybrid work arrangements, has enhanced its relevance and impact. The principles outlined by Tannenbaum and Salas have proven applicable to virtual teams, providing guidance for organizations navigating the challenges of distributed collaboration.

## Detailed Theme Analysis

### Theme 1: Team Composition and Member Selection
**Explanation:** The authors emphasize that effective teams begin with thoughtful composition, focusing not just on individual competencies but on how team members' skills, knowledge, and attributes complement each other. This driver challenges the common practice of assembling teams based on availability or organizational politics rather than strategic consideration of what the team needs to accomplish. The framework provides specific guidance on balancing technical expertise, collaborative skills, and diversity of perspectives while considering team size and role clarity.

**Manifestation in Book:** The authors present detailed criteria for team member selection, including assessment tools for evaluating both technical competencies and teamwork skills. They provide examples of how successful organizations approach team composition systematically, including case studies of teams that failed due to poor composition decisions and others that succeeded through strategic member selection. The book includes specific guidance on optimal team size for different types of tasks and how to balance expertise with collaborative capability.

### Theme 2: Clear Team Purpose and Goals
**Explanation:** This driver focuses on the critical importance of shared understanding about why the team exists and what it is trying to accomplish. The authors argue that many team failures can be traced to ambiguous or conflicting goals, and that high-performing teams invest significant effort in clarifying and aligning around their purpose. This goes beyond simply stating objectives to creating genuine shared mental models about priorities, success criteria, and the team's role within the larger organizational context.

**Manifestation in Book:** Tannenbaum and Salas provide frameworks for goal-setting that go beyond traditional SMART criteria to include alignment mechanisms and regular recalibration processes. They offer specific examples of teams that struggled with competing priorities and demonstrate how successful teams create and maintain goal clarity through structured processes. The book includes tools for assessing goal clarity and techniques for facilitating goal alignment discussions.

### Theme 3: Effective Team Leadership
**Explanation:** The authors present leadership as a critical driver that can be shared among team members rather than residing solely with a designated leader. This driver encompasses both task leadership (focusing on goal achievement and performance) and team maintenance leadership (focusing on relationships and team cohesion). The framework emphasizes adaptive leadership that responds to changing team needs and situational demands, moving beyond traditional command-and-control approaches to more facilitative and empowering leadership styles.

**Manifestation in Book:** The book provides detailed descriptions of leadership behaviors that correlate with team effectiveness, including specific examples of how leaders can facilitate team processes, manage conflict, and create psychological safety. The authors present case studies of teams with different leadership structures, from traditional hierarchical arrangements to shared leadership models, demonstrating how leadership effectiveness varies based on context and team maturity.

### Theme 4: Communication and Information Sharing
**Explanation:** This driver addresses both the quantity and quality of communication within teams, emphasizing that effective teams develop systematic approaches to information sharing that go beyond informal exchanges. The authors highlight the importance of creating communication norms, establishing feedback mechanisms, and ensuring that critical information reaches the right people at the right time. This includes both task-focused communication and relationship-building communication that maintains team cohesion.

**Manifestation in Book:** The authors provide specific examples of communication breakdowns that led to team failures and contrast these with teams that developed robust communication systems. They offer practical tools for improving team communication, including structured meeting formats, feedback protocols, and information-sharing technologies. The book includes assessment instruments for evaluating communication effectiveness and intervention strategies for addressing communication problems.

### Theme 5: Coordination and Collaboration Processes
**Explanation:** This driver focuses on how team members organize their work and coordinate their efforts to achieve collective goals. The authors emphasize that effective coordination requires both explicit processes (such as project management systems and role definitions) and implicit coordination (such as shared mental models and anticipatory behavior). This driver recognizes that as teams become more experienced and skilled, they can rely more on implicit coordination, but that explicit processes remain important for complex or novel tasks.

**Manifestation in Book:** Tannenbaum and Salas provide detailed examples of coordination mechanisms used by high-performing teams across different industries, from surgical teams in hospitals to software development teams in technology companies. They offer frameworks for designing coordination processes that match task complexity and team experience levels, including specific tools for workflow design and role clarification.

### Theme 6: Team Learning and Adaptation
**Explanation:** The authors identify continuous learning and adaptation as essential capabilities for sustained team effectiveness. This driver encompasses both learning from experience (including failures and successes) and proactive learning to prepare for future challenges. The framework emphasizes the importance of creating psychological safety that enables team members to acknowledge mistakes, share concerns, and experiment with new approaches without fear of punishment or embarrassment.

**Manifestation in Book:** The book provides extensive examples of teams that successfully adapted to changing circumstances and those that failed to learn from their experiences. The authors offer specific techniques for facilitating team learning, including after-action reviews, scenario planning, and structured reflection processes. They demonstrate how learning-oriented teams develop resilience and improve their performance over time.

### Theme 7: Organizational Support and Context
**Explanation:** This driver recognizes that teams operate within organizational systems that can either support or undermine their effectiveness. The authors emphasize that even teams with strong internal dynamics will struggle if they lack adequate resources, face conflicting organizational pressures, or operate within cultures that don't value collaboration. This driver includes both tangible support (such as resources and tools) and intangible support (such as organizational culture and reward systems).

**Manifestation in Book:** The authors provide detailed analysis of how organizational factors influence team performance, including case studies of teams that succeeded despite organizational obstacles and others that failed due to lack of organizational support. They offer guidance for senior leaders on creating team-supportive environments and for team leaders on navigating organizational constraints while maintaining team effectiveness.

### Theme 8: Performance Monitoring and Feedback
**Explanation:** The final driver focuses on the systematic assessment of team performance and the provision of feedback that enables continuous improvement. The authors argue that effective teams develop both formal and informal mechanisms for monitoring their progress, identifying problems early, and making necessary adjustments. This includes both outcome-focused metrics (measuring results) and process-focused metrics (measuring how well the team is functioning).

**Manifestation in Book:** Tannenbaum and Salas provide comprehensive frameworks for team performance measurement, including balanced scorecards that capture multiple dimensions of team effectiveness. They offer specific examples of teams that used performance monitoring to identify and address problems before they became critical, as well as teams that failed due to inadequate performance tracking. The book includes practical tools for establishing performance monitoring systems and creating feedback loops that support continuous improvement.
