# Theory in Practice: Increasing professional Effectiveness
**Author:** Argyris, Schone

**Tags:** Managing, OD

## Summary

"Theory in Practice: Increasing Professional Effectiveness" by Chris Argyris and Donald Schön is a groundbreaking work that explores the gap between what professionals say they do (espoused theory) and what they actually do (theory-in-use). Published in 1974, this seminal book introduces revolutionary concepts about organizational learning, professional development, and the barriers that prevent individuals and organizations from achieving genuine effectiveness.

The central premise of the book is that most professionals operate under two distinct theories of action. Their espoused theory represents their stated beliefs, values, and intended behaviors - what they claim guides their actions. However, their theory-in-use represents the actual theories that govern their behavior, which can be inferred from observing their actions rather than listening to their words. This disconnect creates significant problems in professional effectiveness and organizational learning.

Argyris and Schön identify two primary models of theory-in-use that dominate professional behavior. Model I is characterized by the desire to remain in unilateral control, maximize winning while minimizing losing, suppress negative feelings, and behave according to what is considered rational. While these behaviors may seem logical, they actually create defensive routines, reduce learning, and decrease long-term effectiveness. Most professionals operate under Model I without realizing it, believing they are being helpful and effective while actually creating counterproductive dynamics.

Model II represents an alternative approach focused on valid information, free and informed choice, and internal commitment to decisions. This model encourages inquiry, reduces defensiveness, and promotes genuine learning. However, transitioning from Model I to Model II requires significant personal and organizational change, as it challenges deeply held assumptions about effectiveness and control.

The authors introduce the crucial distinction between single-loop and double-loop learning. Single-loop learning occurs when errors are detected and corrected without questioning the underlying assumptions, policies, or objectives. It's like a thermostat that maintains temperature without questioning whether the current setting is appropriate. Double-loop learning, however, involves questioning the fundamental assumptions and values that guide behavior. It asks not just "How can we do this better?" but "Should we be doing this at all?"

The book demonstrates how defensive routines - systematic ways of dealing with threat or embarrassment - prevent double-loop learning and maintain the status quo. These routines are often undiscussable, and their undiscussability is also undiscussable, creating what the authors call "organizational defensive loops." These patterns become self-sealing, making it extremely difficult for individuals and organizations to recognize and change counterproductive behaviors.

Through detailed case studies and interventions, Argyris and Schön show how professionals can develop greater self-awareness and effectiveness. They present methods for surfacing and examining theories-in-use, including the use of directly observable data, the ladder of inference, and action maps. These tools help individuals recognize the gap between their intentions and their actual impact on others.

The book's significance extends far beyond individual professional development. It provides a framework for understanding why organizational change efforts often fail, why well-intentioned people create unintended consequences, and why learning in organizations is so difficult to achieve. The concepts have influenced fields ranging from management consulting and organizational development to education and public policy.

The work challenges the traditional approach to professional education, which typically focuses on technical skills and espoused theories while ignoring the theories-in-use that actually govern behavior. The authors argue that true professional effectiveness requires the ability to reflect on and modify one's theory-in-use, not just acquire new technical knowledge.

"Theory in Practice" remains highly relevant in today's complex organizational environment, where collaboration, innovation, and adaptive learning are essential for success. The book provides both a diagnostic framework for understanding professional ineffectiveness and a prescriptive approach for developing genuine competence. It emphasizes that increasing professional effectiveness is not just about learning new techniques but about developing the capacity for continuous learning and self-reflection.

## Theme Analysis

### Theme 1: Espoused Theory vs. Theory-in-Use
**Explanation:** This fundamental distinction reveals the gap between what people say they believe and how they actually behave. Most professionals are unaware of this discrepancy, which creates significant barriers to effectiveness and learning.
**Examples:** The book presents cases where managers claim to value participation and openness but actually control conversations and suppress dissent. Consultants who espouse collaborative approaches while unilaterally diagnosing client problems demonstrate this gap.

### Theme 2: Model I and Model II Theories of Action
**Explanation:** These represent two fundamentally different approaches to professional behavior. Model I emphasizes control and winning, while Model II focuses on learning and mutual inquiry, leading to vastly different outcomes in professional effectiveness.
**Examples:** Model I behaviors include advocating positions without inquiry, making attributions about others' motives without testing them, and controlling tasks unilaterally. Model II involves combining advocacy with inquiry, testing assumptions publicly, and sharing control of tasks.

### Theme 3: Single-Loop vs. Double-Loop Learning
**Explanation:** Single-loop learning addresses symptoms without questioning underlying assumptions, while double-loop learning examines and potentially changes the fundamental values and policies that guide behavior. Double-loop learning is essential for genuine organizational change.
**Examples:** A company improving efficiency without questioning whether they're making the right products exemplifies single-loop learning. Double-loop learning would involve questioning the entire business strategy and market assumptions.

### Theme 4: Defensive Routines and Organizational Learning
**Explanation:** Systematic patterns of behavior designed to protect individuals and organizations from threat or embarrassment actually prevent learning and perpetuate problems. These routines become self-reinforcing and difficult to change.
**Examples:** The book describes how organizations develop "undiscussable" topics, such as a CEO's management style or a department's poor performance, and how the undiscussability itself becomes undiscussable, creating self-sealing loops.

### Theme 5: Action Science and Intervention Methods
**Explanation:** The authors present specific methodologies for surfacing and examining theories-in-use, enabling individuals and organizations to develop greater self-awareness and effectiveness through systematic inquiry and reflection.
**Examples:** Techniques include creating action maps to diagram behavior patterns, using the ladder of inference to understand reasoning processes, and conducting interventions that help people see the gap between their intentions and their actual impact.

### Theme 6: Professional Education and Development
**Explanation:** Traditional professional education focuses on technical skills and espoused theories while ignoring the behavioral skills needed to implement knowledge effectively. True professional development requires attention to theories-in-use.
**Examples:** The book critiques MBA programs and professional training that teach analytical frameworks without helping students understand how their interpersonal behavior affects their ability to apply these frameworks in real organizational settings.
