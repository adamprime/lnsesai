# Why We Do What We Do
**Author:** Deci

**Tags:** Motivation

## Summary

"Why We Do What We Do: Understanding Self-Motivation" by Edward L. Deci (co-authored with Richard Flaste) presents a groundbreaking exploration of human motivation based on decades of psychological research. As one of the world's foremost experts on self-motivation, Deci challenges conventional wisdom about what drives human behavior and offers a scientifically-grounded alternative to traditional carrot-and-stick approaches to motivation.

The book's central argument revolves around Self-Determination Theory (SDT), which Deci developed through extensive research. This theory distinguishes between intrinsic motivation—the drive that comes from within when we find activities inherently satisfying—and extrinsic motivation—behavior driven by external rewards or punishments. Deci demonstrates that intrinsic motivation is not only more sustainable but also leads to greater well-being, creativity, and performance.

A key revelation in the book is that external rewards can actually undermine intrinsic motivation, a phenomenon Deci calls the "undermining effect." Through numerous studies, he shows how offering rewards for activities people already enjoy can diminish their natural interest in those activities. This finding has profound implications for parenting, education, and workplace management, suggesting that many common motivational strategies may be counterproductive.

Deci identifies three fundamental psychological needs that fuel intrinsic motivation: autonomy, competence, and relatedness. Autonomy refers to the need to feel volitional and self-directed in one's actions. Competence involves the need to feel effective and capable of achieving desired outcomes. Relatedness encompasses the need for connection and belonging with others. When these needs are satisfied, people naturally engage in activities with enthusiasm and commitment.

The book extensively examines how different environments either support or thwart these basic needs. In educational settings, Deci shows how autonomy-supportive teaching methods—those that provide choice, acknowledge students' perspectives, and minimize controlling language—lead to better learning outcomes and greater student engagement compared to controlling approaches. Similarly, in workplace contexts, managers who support employee autonomy through meaningful work, participative decision-making, and recognition of individual perspectives create more motivated and productive teams.

Deci also explores the role of feedback in motivation, distinguishing between informational feedback that supports competence and controlling feedback that undermines autonomy. Informational feedback helps people understand their progress and improve their skills, while controlling feedback focuses on compliance and comparison with others. The former enhances intrinsic motivation, while the latter can diminish it.

The book addresses practical applications across various life domains. In parenting, Deci advocates for setting limits while still supporting children's autonomy, helping them understand the reasons behind rules rather than simply demanding compliance. In education, he promotes student-centered learning approaches that foster curiosity and self-direction. In organizations, he recommends creating environments that provide meaningful work, opportunities for growth, and genuine participation in decision-making.

Deci also examines the relationship between motivation and well-being, showing how intrinsically motivated behavior leads to greater life satisfaction and psychological health. People who are primarily driven by intrinsic goals—such as personal growth, relationships, and community contribution—report higher levels of well-being than those focused on extrinsic goals like wealth, fame, or image.

The significance of this book lies in its challenge to deeply ingrained assumptions about human nature and motivation. By demonstrating that people are naturally inclined toward growth, learning, and contribution when their basic psychological needs are met, Deci offers a more optimistic and effective approach to motivation than traditional behaviorist models. His research has influenced fields ranging from education and healthcare to business and sports psychology.

"Why We Do What We Do" provides both theoretical understanding and practical tools for creating environments that foster genuine motivation. It offers a roadmap for parents, educators, managers, and individuals seeking to understand and nurture the conditions that allow human potential to flourish. The book's enduring relevance stems from its foundation in rigorous scientific research combined with accessible explanations and real-world applications that can transform how we approach motivation in all areas of life.

## Theme Analysis

### Theme 1: Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic Motivation
**Explanation:** Deci establishes the fundamental distinction between motivation that comes from within (intrinsic) and motivation driven by external factors (extrinsic). This core concept demonstrates that intrinsic motivation leads to more sustainable engagement, creativity, and well-being than external rewards or punishments.
**Examples:** The book presents studies showing how children who receive rewards for drawing become less interested in drawing when rewards are removed, and how students perform better when driven by curiosity rather than grades.

### Theme 2: The Three Basic Psychological Needs
**Explanation:** Autonomy, competence, and relatedness form the foundation of human motivation according to Self-Determination Theory. When these needs are satisfied, people naturally engage in growth-oriented behavior and experience greater well-being.
**Examples:** Deci illustrates how workplace environments that provide choice (autonomy), skill development opportunities (competence), and team connection (relatedness) create more engaged employees than those relying solely on financial incentives.

### Theme 3: The Undermining Effect of Rewards
**Explanation:** External rewards can actually diminish intrinsic motivation, particularly when they are perceived as controlling rather than informational. This counterintuitive finding challenges traditional incentive-based approaches to motivation.
**Examples:** The book describes experiments where people paid to solve puzzles showed less interest in continuing the activity during free time compared to those who solved puzzles without payment, demonstrating how rewards can backfire.

### Theme 4: Autonomy-Supportive vs. Controlling Environments
**Explanation:** Environments that support people's sense of choice and self-direction foster intrinsic motivation, while controlling environments that emphasize compliance and external pressure undermine it. The way authority figures communicate and structure situations significantly impacts motivation.
**Examples:** Deci contrasts teachers who use controlling language ("you should," "you must") with those who provide rationale and choice, showing how the latter approach leads to better learning outcomes and student engagement.

### Theme 5: The Role of Feedback in Motivation
**Explanation:** Feedback can either enhance or diminish motivation depending on whether it's perceived as informational (supporting competence) or controlling (pressuring compliance). The quality and delivery of feedback significantly impacts how people respond to it.
**Examples:** The book distinguishes between feedback that helps people improve their skills versus feedback that compares them to others or emphasizes external evaluation, showing how informational feedback maintains intrinsic motivation.

### Theme 6: Motivation and Well-being Connection
**Explanation:** Intrinsic motivation is closely linked to psychological well-being and life satisfaction. People who pursue intrinsic goals and engage in self-determined behavior report higher levels of happiness and fulfillment than those driven primarily by external factors.
**Examples:** Deci presents research showing that individuals focused on intrinsic goals like personal growth and relationships experience greater well-being than those pursuing extrinsic goals like wealth or fame, even when they achieve those external objectives.
