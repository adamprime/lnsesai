## Comprehensive Summary

"Teaching Thinking Skills: Theory and Practice," edited by Joan Boykoff Baron and Robert J. Sternberg, stands as a seminal work in the field of cognitive education, published in 1987 during a critical period of educational reform in the United States. This comprehensive volume emerged during the height of the "thinking skills movement" of the 1980s, when educators and policymakers were increasingly concerned about American students' performance in critical thinking, problem-solving, and higher-order cognitive tasks compared to their international peers.

The book represents a collaborative effort bringing together leading researchers, theorists, and practitioners in cognitive psychology and education to address fundamental questions about whether thinking skills can be taught, how they should be taught, and what constitutes effective instruction in this domain. The editors assembled contributions from prominent figures including Richard Paul, Matthew Lipman, Arthur Whimbey, David Perkins, and others who were pioneering various approaches to thinking skills instruction.

**Historical and Educational Context**

The work emerged during a period of significant educational concern in America, following reports like "A Nation at Risk" (1983) that highlighted deficiencies in American education. There was growing recognition that traditional educational approaches, which emphasized rote memorization and basic skill acquisition, were insufficient for preparing students for an increasingly complex, information-rich society. The book addresses this context by examining whether schools could systematically develop students' capacity for critical thinking, creative problem-solving, and metacognitive awareness.

The volume reflects the intellectual climate of the 1980s, when cognitive psychology was providing new insights into the nature of human thinking and learning. Researchers were beginning to understand thinking not as a fixed capacity but as a set of learnable skills and strategies. This represented a significant shift from earlier views that treated intelligence and thinking ability as largely innate and unchangeable.

**Key Theoretical Frameworks and Approaches**

The book presents multiple theoretical frameworks for understanding and teaching thinking skills. One central framework distinguishes between different types of thinking skills programs: those that teach thinking skills as separate subjects (such as Philosophy for Children or CoRT), those that infuse thinking skills instruction into existing subject matter, and those that immerse students in thinking-rich environments without explicit instruction in thinking processes.

Sternberg's triarchic theory of intelligence provides another key framework, distinguishing between analytical, creative, and practical thinking skills. This theory suggests that effective thinking skills instruction must address all three components rather than focusing solely on analytical reasoning, as many traditional programs did. The book explores how different instructional approaches align with or address these various components of intelligent thinking.

The volume also presents metacognitive frameworks, emphasizing the importance of teaching students not just how to think, but how to think about their thinking. This includes developing students' awareness of their own cognitive processes, their ability to monitor their thinking during problem-solving, and their capacity to regulate and adjust their cognitive strategies based on task demands and performance feedback.

**Methodological Approaches and Instructional Strategies**

The book examines various methodological approaches to thinking skills instruction, each with distinct theoretical foundations and practical implementations. The Philosophy for Children program, developed by Matthew Lipman, uses philosophical dialogue and inquiry to develop students' reasoning skills, critical thinking abilities, and capacity for logical argumentation. This approach embeds thinking skills development within engaging narratives and community of inquiry discussions.

The Instrumental Enrichment program, developed by Reuven Feuerstein, takes a different approach, using abstract exercises designed to develop specific cognitive functions such as spatial orientation, temporal relations, and logical reasoning. This program is based on the theory of structural cognitive modifiability, which holds that cognitive abilities can be significantly enhanced through appropriate mediated learning experiences.

The book also examines subject-specific approaches to thinking skills instruction, exploring how thinking skills can be developed within mathematics, science, social studies, and language arts. These approaches recognize that thinking skills are often domain-specific and that effective instruction must consider the unique demands and characteristics of different academic disciplines.

**Assessment and Evaluation Challenges**

A significant portion of the volume addresses the complex challenges of assessing thinking skills and evaluating the effectiveness of thinking skills programs. The authors grapple with fundamental questions about what constitutes evidence of improved thinking and how such improvements can be reliably measured. Traditional standardized tests, they argue, are often inadequate for assessing higher-order thinking skills, leading to the need for more sophisticated assessment approaches including performance-based assessments, portfolio evaluations, and authentic assessment tasks.

The book examines various research studies attempting to evaluate thinking skills programs, highlighting both promising results and methodological limitations. The authors emphasize the importance of long-term follow-up studies and the need to assess transfer of thinking skills to new contexts and domains, which represents one of the most challenging aspects of thinking skills education.
