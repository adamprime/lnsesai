# Made to Stick
**Author:** Heath

**Tags:** Communication, Storytelling

## Summary

"Made to Stick" by brothers Chip Heath and Dan Heath explores why some ideas survive and thrive while others fade into obscurity. The book addresses a fundamental communication challenge: how to craft messages that are not only heard but remembered, understood, and acted upon over time. Drawing from extensive research in psychology, sociology, and communication theory, the Heath brothers identify six core principles that make ideas "sticky" – meaning they adhere to people's minds and influence behavior long after the initial encounter.

The central framework of the book is encapsulated in the acronym SUCCESS, representing six key principles: Simple, Unexpected, Concrete, Credible, Emotional, and Stories. Each principle addresses a specific barrier to effective communication and provides actionable strategies for overcoming it.

**Simplicity** involves distilling ideas to their essential core, stripping away unnecessary complexity while preserving the most important message. The authors emphasize that simplicity isn't about dumbing down content but rather about finding the single most important thing to communicate. They illustrate this with examples like Southwest Airlines' focus on being "the low-cost airline" – a simple concept that guides every business decision.

**Unexpectedness** captures attention by violating expectations and creating curiosity gaps. The Heath brothers explain how surprise opens people's minds to new information, but the surprise must be relevant to the core message. They demonstrate this principle through examples like flight safety videos that use humor or unusual scenarios to maintain passenger attention.

**Concreteness** makes abstract concepts tangible and relatable. The authors argue that concrete language and imagery help people understand and remember ideas because our brains are wired to process specific, sensory information more effectively than abstract concepts. They contrast vague mission statements with concrete goals like John F. Kennedy's commitment to "landing a man on the moon and returning him safely to Earth."

**Credibility** establishes trust and believability through various means: external authorities, internal consistency, vivid details, or statistics that resonate. The book explores how ideas gain credibility not just through expert endorsement but through authentic details and relatable sources that audiences can verify or connect with personally.

**Emotional** connection drives people to care about ideas. The Heath brothers distinguish between analytical thinking and emotional engagement, arguing that while analysis might convince people intellectually, emotions motivate action. They explore how successful campaigns and movements tap into fundamental human values and feelings rather than relying solely on logical arguments.

**Stories** serve as mental flight simulators, allowing people to experience ideas vicariously and understand how to apply them in their own contexts. The authors identify different types of stories – challenge plots, connection plots, and creativity plots – each serving different persuasive purposes and helping audiences internalize messages through narrative structure.

Throughout the book, the Heath brothers address the "Curse of Knowledge" – the cognitive bias that makes it difficult for experts to communicate with novices because they cannot easily recreate the mental state of not knowing what they know. This curse explains why many well-intentioned communications fail: the communicator assumes too much background knowledge and uses language or concepts that are inaccessible to their audience.

The significance of "Made to Stick" extends beyond marketing and advertising into education, leadership, public policy, and personal communication. The book provides a systematic approach to crafting messages that cut through information overload and create lasting impact. In an era of constant communication and shortened attention spans, the principles offer a research-based methodology for ensuring important ideas receive the attention and retention they deserve.

The book's practical applications span multiple domains: teachers can make lessons more memorable, leaders can communicate vision more effectively, public health officials can design better awareness campaigns, and individuals can improve their everyday communication. By understanding and applying these six principles, communicators can increase the likelihood that their ideas will not only be heard but will stick in minds and influence behavior over time.

## Theme Analysis

### Theme 1: The Curse of Knowledge
**Explanation:** This cognitive bias prevents experts from effectively communicating with novices because they cannot easily remember what it was like not to know their subject matter. It's the primary barrier to creating sticky ideas and explains why many well-intentioned communications fail.
**Examples:** The book illustrates this through examples of technical jargon in user manuals, overly complex academic presentations, and business communications filled with insider terminology that alienates rather than informs audiences.

### Theme 2: Simplicity as Strategic Focus
**Explanation:** True simplicity involves identifying and communicating the single most important core of an idea, not oversimplifying complex topics. It requires ruthless prioritization and the discipline to say no to secondary messages that might dilute the primary point.
**Examples:** Southwest Airlines' "low-cost airline" positioning, military communications that prioritize the most critical information, and successful advertising slogans that capture brand essence in memorable phrases.

### Theme 3: Attention Through Surprise and Curiosity
**Explanation:** Unexpected elements capture attention by creating surprise, but sustainable engagement requires opening curiosity gaps that audiences want to fill. The surprise must be relevant to the core message rather than gratuitous.
**Examples:** Creative safety demonstrations that use humor while maintaining safety focus, news stories that begin with counterintuitive statistics, and presentations that start with surprising facts that connect to the main argument.

### Theme 4: Concrete Communication Over Abstraction
**Explanation:** Human brains process concrete, sensory information more effectively than abstract concepts. Making ideas tangible through specific details, vivid imagery, and relatable examples dramatically improves comprehension and retention.
**Examples:** Kennedy's moon landing goal versus vague "space exploration," specific customer stories rather than general market research, and teaching methods that use physical demonstrations alongside theoretical explanations.

### Theme 5: Emotional Engagement as Action Catalyst
**Explanation:** While analytical arguments might convince people intellectually, emotional connections motivate actual behavior change. Successful sticky ideas tap into fundamental human values and feelings that drive action beyond mere understanding.
**Examples:** Charity campaigns that focus on individual stories rather than statistics, environmental messages that connect to personal values, and leadership communications that inspire through shared vision and purpose.

### Theme 6: Stories as Mental Simulation
**Explanation:** Stories function as practice sessions for the brain, allowing audiences to experience situations vicariously and understand how to apply ideas in their own contexts. Different story types serve different persuasive and educational purposes.
**Examples:** Case studies in business education, parables that teach moral lessons, success stories that demonstrate possibility, and cautionary tales that illustrate consequences of poor decisions.
