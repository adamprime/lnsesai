# Intelligence Reframed
**Author:** Howard Gardner

**Tags:** Thinking

## Comprehensive Summary

**Intelligence Reframed: Multiple Intelligences for the 21st Century** represents Howard Gardner's comprehensive update and expansion of his groundbreaking theory of multiple intelligences, originally introduced in his 1983 work *Frames of Mind*. Published in 1999, this book serves as both a progress report on how the theory has evolved and been applied over nearly two decades, and a forward-looking examination of intelligence for the new millennium.

**Overview and Key Takeaways**

Gardner's central thesis challenges the traditional notion of intelligence as a single, measurable cognitive capacity typically assessed through IQ tests. Instead, he argues that human intelligence is pluralistic, consisting of multiple, relatively independent intelligences that work together in complex ways. The book presents intelligence as a bio-psychological potential to process information in certain ways to solve problems or create products that are valued in one or more cultural contexts.

The work goes beyond merely restating the original theory by introducing three new intelligences: naturalist intelligence (the ability to recognize and categorize natural phenomena), spiritual intelligence (though Gardner ultimately rejects this as not meeting his criteria), and existential intelligence (the capacity to ponder fundamental questions about existence, meaning, and purpose). This expansion demonstrates the theory's continued evolution and Gardner's willingness to refine his framework based on new evidence and understanding.

**Setting and Context**

The book emerges from the educational and psychological landscape of the late 20th century, a period marked by increasing dissatisfaction with standardized testing and traditional educational approaches. Gardner writes against the backdrop of the "testing culture" that dominated American education, where intelligence was narrowly defined and measured. The historical context includes the rise of cognitive science, neuroscience advances, and growing awareness of cultural diversity in learning styles and capabilities.

The social context is equally important: Gardner addresses concerns about educational equity, the need for personalized learning approaches, and the recognition that different cultures value different types of intelligence. The book reflects the broader movement toward more inclusive and comprehensive understandings of human potential that characterized educational reform efforts of the 1990s.

**Key Frameworks and Concepts**

Gardner employs several sophisticated frameworks to support his argument. First, he establishes eight criteria for identifying an intelligence, including potential isolation by brain damage, the existence of savants and prodigies, an identifiable core operation, a distinctive developmental history, evolutionary plausibility, support from experimental psychology, support from psychometric findings, and susceptibility to encoding in a symbol system.

The author utilizes a bio-psychological framework that draws from neuroscience, developmental psychology, and anthropology. He examines how different intelligences manifest across cultures and throughout human development, providing evidence from brain imaging studies, cases of individuals with brain damage, and cross-cultural research. This interdisciplinary approach strengthens his argument by showing convergent evidence from multiple fields.

Gardner also employs what might be called a "practical application framework," demonstrating how multiple intelligence theory has been implemented in educational settings, business contexts, and personal development programs. He provides detailed case studies and examples of schools that have successfully integrated MI theory into their curricula, showing both successes and challenges in real-world applications.

**The Eight (Plus) Intelligences**

The book details the original seven intelligences: linguistic, logical-mathematical, spatial, bodily-kinesthetic, musical, interpersonal, and intrapersonal. Gardner provides updated research and examples for each, showing how they manifest in different individuals and cultures. The addition of naturalist intelligence reflects his recognition of abilities that were previously undervalued in educational settings but are crucial for understanding and interacting with the natural world.

Gardner's consideration and ultimate rejection of spiritual intelligence demonstrates his rigorous approach to theory development. He explains that while spiritual concerns are important, they don't meet his criteria for a distinct intelligence because they cannot be localized in specific brain regions and lack the other hallmarks of his framework.

**Significance and Impact**

*Intelligence Reframed* has had profound influence on educational theory and practice worldwide. The book has been translated into numerous languages and has inspired educational reforms in countries across the globe. Its impact extends beyond education into business training, personal development, and therapeutic interventions.

The book's reception has been generally positive among educators and practitioners, though it has faced criticism from some psychologists who argue for the continued relevance of general intelligence (g-factor). Critics contend that Gardner's intelligences might be better understood as talents or abilities rather than intelligences per se. However, supporters argue that the theory has democratized the concept of intelligence and provided valuable frameworks for understanding human diversity.

The lasting influence of *Intelligence Reframed* can be seen in contemporary educational practices such as differentiated instruction, project-based learning, and portfolio assessment. The book has contributed to a broader cultural shift away from narrow definitions of academic success toward more holistic understandings of human potential. It has also influenced policy discussions about educational assessment and has provided theoretical support for critics of high-stakes standardized testing.

## Detailed Theme Analysis

### Theme 1: Pluralistic View of Intelligence
**Explanation:** Gardner fundamentally challenges the traditional unitary view of intelligence, arguing instead for a pluralistic model where multiple intelligences operate relatively independently. This theme represents the core revolutionary aspect of his theory, suggesting that intelligence cannot be captured by a single number or test score. The pluralistic view recognizes that individuals can be highly intelligent in some areas while average or below average in others, fundamentally changing how we understand human cognitive capacity. This perspective has profound implications for education, assessment, and how society values different types of human abilities.

**Manifestation in Book:** Gardner provides extensive examples of individuals who excel in one intelligence while struggling in others, such as savants who demonstrate extraordinary musical or mathematical abilities despite significant limitations in other areas. He discusses how different cultures value different intelligences, with some societies prizing linguistic abilities while others emphasize spatial or bodily-kinesthetic intelligence. The book includes case studies of students who were considered "learning disabled" under traditional measures but showed remarkable abilities when assessed through multiple intelligence frameworks.

### Theme 2: Cultural Relativity of Intelligence
**Explanation:** Gardner emphasizes that intelligence cannot be understood apart from cultural context, arguing that what counts as intelligent behavior varies significantly across different societies and historical periods. This theme challenges Western-centric views of intelligence and highlights how cultural values shape both the development and recognition of different intelligences. The cultural relativity theme suggests that educational systems should reflect the values and needs of their specific cultural contexts rather than imposing universal standards. This perspective has important implications for multicultural education and the assessment of students from diverse backgrounds.

**Manifestation in Book:** The book provides examples of how navigation skills are highly valued and developed in Pacific Island cultures, representing a form of spatial intelligence that might not be recognized in Western educational systems. Gardner discusses how oral traditions in some cultures develop linguistic intelligence in ways that differ from literate societies' emphasis on written language. He examines how different cultures create different "crystallizing experiences" that help individuals discover and develop their particular intelligences.

### Theme 3: Neurobiological Foundations
**Explanation:** Gardner grounds his theory in neuroscience research, arguing that different intelligences correspond to different neural networks and brain regions. This theme provides biological credibility to the multiple intelligence theory by showing that intelligence has physical substrates in the brain. The neurobiological foundation helps distinguish Gardner's theory from earlier theories that were based primarily on statistical analysis of test scores. This approach also allows for the possibility that brain damage could selectively impair some intelligences while leaving others intact, providing a natural experiment for understanding the independence of different intelligences.

**Manifestation in Book:** Gardner presents cases of individuals with brain damage who lose specific abilities while retaining others, such as musicians who can no longer speak but can still compose music, or individuals who lose mathematical ability while retaining linguistic skills. He discusses research on brain imaging that shows different patterns of activation for different types of cognitive tasks. The book examines how different intelligences have different developmental trajectories, with some peaking early in life while others continue to develop throughout adulthood.

### Theme 4: Educational Applications and Reform
**Explanation:** A major theme of the book is how multiple intelligence theory can transform educational practice by providing more personalized and effective approaches to teaching and learning. Gardner argues that schools should assess students' intelligence profiles and design curricula that engage multiple intelligences rather than focusing primarily on linguistic and logical-mathematical abilities. This theme represents the practical application of the theory and demonstrates its potential for creating more inclusive and effective educational environments. The educational reform theme also addresses how assessment practices should change to reflect the full range of human intelligences.

**Manifestation in Book:** Gardner provides detailed case studies of schools that have implemented MI-based curricula, showing how students who previously struggled began to succeed when taught through their stronger intelligences. He discusses project-based learning approaches that allow students to demonstrate understanding through multiple modalities. The book examines how portfolio assessment can capture student abilities that traditional tests miss, and how teachers can be trained to recognize and nurture different intelligences in their students.

### Theme 5: Individual Differences and Profiles
**Explanation:** Gardner emphasizes that each person has a unique profile of intelligences, with different strengths and weaknesses across the various domains. This theme challenges the notion that people can be ranked along a single dimension of intelligence and instead suggests that human cognitive abilities are as individual as fingerprints. The individual differences theme has important implications for career guidance, educational planning, and personal development. It suggests that rather than trying to remediate weaknesses, individuals and educators should focus on identifying and building upon strengths.

**Manifestation in Book:** The book provides examples of famous individuals who demonstrated exceptional abilities in specific intelligences, such as Einstein's spatial intelligence, Mozart's musical intelligence, or Gandhi's interpersonal intelligence. Gardner discusses how intelligence profiles can change over time and how individuals can develop strategies to work around their weaker areas while capitalizing on their strengths. He examines how understanding one's intelligence profile can lead to better career choices and more satisfying life paths.

### Theme 6: Assessment and Testing Critique
**Explanation:** Gardner presents a sustained critique of traditional intelligence testing, arguing that IQ tests capture only a narrow slice of human cognitive abilities and fail to predict success in real-world contexts. This theme challenges the dominance of standardized testing in education and calls for more authentic forms of assessment that can capture the full range of human intelligences. The assessment critique theme is particularly relevant in the context of high-stakes testing and educational accountability movements. Gardner argues for assessment approaches that are more closely tied to meaningful activities and real-world applications of intelligence.

**Manifestation in Book:** Gardner discusses the limitations of paper-and-pencil tests in capturing abilities like musical, bodily-kinesthetic, or interpersonal intelligence. He provides examples of alternative assessment approaches, such as observing students engaged in authentic tasks or having them create portfolios that demonstrate their abilities across multiple domains. The book examines how traditional tests may be culturally biased and fail to recognize forms of intelligence that are valued in non-Western cultures.

### Theme 7: Developmental Perspectives
**Explanation:** The book emphasizes that intelligences develop over time and that different intelligences have different developmental trajectories and sensitive periods. This theme suggests that educational interventions should be timed appropriately and that individuals may discover and develop their intelligences at different life stages. The developmental perspective also highlights the importance of providing rich environments and experiences that can nurture the growth of different intelligences. This theme has implications for early childhood education, talent development programs, and lifelong learning approaches.
