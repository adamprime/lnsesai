# Organizational Learning II
**Author:** Argyris & Schon

**Tags:** Managing Change, OD

## Summary

"Organizational Learning II: Theory, Method, and Practice" by Chris Argyris and Donald Schön represents a seminal advancement in understanding how organizations can develop genuine learning capabilities. Building upon their earlier work, the authors present a sophisticated framework for organizational learning that goes beyond simple adaptation to encompass fundamental transformation of organizational theories-in-use.

The book's central argument revolves around the distinction between single-loop and double-loop learning. Single-loop learning occurs when organizations detect and correct errors within existing frameworks and assumptions, much like a thermostat that maintains temperature within preset parameters. Double-loop learning, however, involves questioning and modifying the underlying assumptions, values, and policies that guide organizational action. This deeper form of learning requires organizations to examine not just what they do, but why they do it and whether their fundamental approaches remain valid.

Argyris and Schön introduce the critical concept of "theories-in-use" versus "espoused theories." Organizations often proclaim certain values and strategies (espoused theories) while actually operating according to different, often unconscious principles (theories-in-use). This gap creates significant barriers to effective learning and change. The authors argue that sustainable organizational learning requires surfacing and examining these theories-in-use, making them explicit and open to inquiry.

The book extensively explores defensive routines—organizational patterns that protect individuals and groups from embarrassment or threat but simultaneously prevent learning. These routines create "undiscussables" and "undiscussable undiscussables," topics that cannot be openly examined despite their critical importance to organizational effectiveness. Such defensive patterns become self-reinforcing, creating what the authors term "organizational learning disabilities."

A key methodological contribution is the concept of "action science," an approach that combines rigorous inquiry with practical intervention. Action science emphasizes the importance of directly observable data, the testing of inferences, and the creation of conditions where organizational members can engage in productive reasoning about difficult issues. This methodology provides concrete tools for practitioners seeking to foster genuine organizational learning.

The authors also examine the role of organizational inquiry systems—formal and informal processes through which organizations gather, process, and act upon information. Effective inquiry systems must be designed to surface and test assumptions, encourage diverse perspectives, and create psychological safety for members to express dissenting views and acknowledge errors.

Throughout the book, Argyris and Schön emphasize that organizational learning is not merely an individual phenomenon scaled up, but requires specific organizational conditions and capabilities. These include leadership that models inquiry and vulnerability, structures that support experimentation and reflection, and cultures that value learning over being right.

The practical implications are profound. Organizations seeking to adapt in rapidly changing environments must develop capabilities for double-loop learning, creating systems and cultures that can question fundamental assumptions and modify core strategies. This requires moving beyond technical solutions to address the human and interpersonal dynamics that often block learning.

The book's significance extends across multiple domains. For organizational development practitioners, it provides both theoretical foundation and practical tools for facilitating deep organizational change. For managers and leaders, it offers insights into the subtle but powerful forces that can undermine learning initiatives and guidance for creating more learning-oriented organizations. For researchers, it establishes a rigorous framework for studying organizational learning phenomena.

The work has influenced decades of subsequent research and practice in organizational learning, knowledge management, and organizational change. Its emphasis on surfacing and testing assumptions has become fundamental to many change methodologies, while its insights into defensive routines have helped explain why many well-intentioned organizational improvements fail to achieve lasting impact.

## Theme Analysis

### Theme 1: Single-Loop vs. Double-Loop Learning
**Explanation:** This foundational distinction separates surface-level error correction from deeper questioning of underlying assumptions and values. Double-loop learning represents the capacity for fundamental organizational transformation rather than mere adaptation.
**Examples:** The book illustrates how organizations might adjust pricing strategies (single-loop) versus questioning their entire business model and value proposition (double-loop) when facing market challenges.

### Theme 2: Theories-in-Use vs. Espoused Theories
**Explanation:** Organizations often operate according to implicit, unexamined principles that differ significantly from their stated values and strategies. This gap creates blind spots and prevents authentic learning and change.
**Examples:** Companies may espouse collaboration and openness while actually rewarding competitive behavior and punishing those who surface problems or challenge decisions.

### Theme 3: Defensive Routines and Organizational Learning Disabilities
**Explanation:** Systematic patterns of behavior protect individuals and groups from threat or embarrassment but create barriers to learning. These routines become self-reinforcing and create "undiscussable" topics that block inquiry.
**Examples:** Meeting dynamics where important issues are discussed only in hallway conversations, or cultures where admitting mistakes is seen as career-limiting, preventing organizations from learning from failures.

### Theme 4: Action Science Methodology
**Explanation:** A rigorous approach to organizational inquiry that combines scientific methods with practical intervention. It emphasizes directly observable data, explicit testing of inferences, and creating conditions for productive reasoning.
**Examples:** The book provides detailed case studies showing how practitioners can use action science techniques to help organizational members examine their reasoning processes and test their assumptions in real-time.

### Theme 5: Organizational Inquiry Systems
**Explanation:** The formal and informal processes through which organizations gather, interpret, and act upon information. Effective inquiry systems must be designed to surface assumptions and encourage diverse perspectives.
**Examples:** The authors examine how different organizational structures and processes either support or inhibit the kind of open inquiry necessary for double-loop learning, including decision-making processes and performance evaluation systems.

### Theme 6: Leadership and Learning
**Explanation:** Leaders play a crucial role in modeling the vulnerability and inquiry necessary for organizational learning. Their behavior either creates psychological safety for learning or reinforces defensive patterns.
**Examples:** The book contrasts leaders who demonstrate curiosity and acknowledge their own errors with those who maintain facades of certainty, showing how leadership behavior directly impacts organizational learning capacity.
