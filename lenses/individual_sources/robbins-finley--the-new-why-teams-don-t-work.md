"The New Why Teams Don't Work: What Goes Wrong and How to Make It Right" by Harvey Robbins and Michael Finley, published in 2000 as an updated version of their original 1995 work, represents a critical examination of team-based organizational structures that became prevalent in the 1990s corporate landscape. The authors, both experienced organizational consultants, wrote this book during a period when American businesses were rapidly adopting team-based approaches as a solution to competitive pressures, downsizing, and the need for increased innovation and efficiency.

**Overview and Key Takeaways**

The central thesis of Robbins and Finley's work challenges the widespread assumption that teams are inherently superior to individual work arrangements. Rather than promoting teams as a universal solution, the authors argue that teams often fail because organizations implement them without understanding their complexity, proper applications, or the significant investment required for success. The book serves as both a cautionary tale and a practical guide, offering a realistic assessment of when teams work, when they don't, and how to maximize their effectiveness when they are the right choice.

The authors identify that the primary reason teams fail is not due to inherent flaws in the team concept, but because of poor implementation, unrealistic expectations, and fundamental misunderstandings about what makes teams effective. They argue that many organizations adopt teams as a fashionable management trend rather than as a strategic response to specific business needs. This leads to what they term "team fatigue" - a phenomenon where employees become cynical about team initiatives due to repeated failures and poorly conceived team implementations.

**Setting and Context**

During this era, traditional hierarchical structures were being questioned, and team-based approaches were seen as a way to flatten organizations, increase employee engagement, and improve responsiveness to market changes.

The authors draw from their extensive consulting experience across various industries, providing examples from manufacturing, service, technology, and healthcare sectors. They emphasize that each stage requires different types of support and intervention from management, and that many teams fail because organizations don't provide appropriate support throughout this cycle.

Another critical framework is their "Team vs. Individual Work Decision Matrix," which helps managers determine when team approaches are appropriate versus when individual work would be more effective. This framework considers factors such as task complexity, interdependence requirements, time constraints, and the availability of team-capable individuals.

The book also introduces the concept of "Team Toxins" - specific behaviors, attitudes, and organizational practices that poison team effectiveness. These include unclear goals, lack of accountability, poor communication systems, inadequate training, and misaligned reward systems. The authors provide detailed strategies for identifying and neutralizing these toxins.

Their "High-Performance Team Characteristics" framework outlines the essential elements that distinguish truly effective teams from merely functional groups.
