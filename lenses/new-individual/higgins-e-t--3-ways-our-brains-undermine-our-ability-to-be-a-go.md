## Summary

"3 Ways Our Brains Undermine Our Ability to Be a Good Leader" by E.T. Higgins, published in Harvard Business Review, explores the neurological barriers that prevent leaders from reaching their full potential. Drawing on research from the NeuroLeadership Institute, the article identifies three core domains essential for leadership success: being future-focused, being good with people, and being able to drive results.

The central premise of Higgins' work is that our brains are naturally wired in ways that can sabotage effective leadership. While these neural patterns may have served evolutionary purposes, they often work against the complex demands of modern leadership. The article argues that understanding these neurological tendencies is crucial for leaders who want to overcome their brain's inherent resistance to excelling across all three critical domains simultaneously.

The first domain, being future-focused, requires leaders to think strategically, anticipate challenges, and maintain a long-term vision while managing immediate concerns. However, our brains are naturally biased toward present-moment thinking and immediate rewards, making sustained future-oriented thinking challenging. This neurological tendency can cause leaders to become reactive rather than proactive, focusing on urgent matters at the expense of important long-term strategic initiatives.

The second domain involves being good with people, which encompasses emotional intelligence, empathy, communication skills, and the ability to build and maintain relationships. The brain's natural self-preservation instincts and cognitive biases can interfere with our ability to truly understand and connect with others. Leaders may struggle with perspective-taking, fall prey to confirmation bias, or allow their own emotional states to cloud their judgment when dealing with team members.

The third domain focuses on driving results, which requires execution, accountability, and the ability to translate vision into action. The brain's tendency toward cognitive overload, decision fatigue, and the preference for familiar patterns can undermine a leader's ability to consistently drive performance and achieve outcomes. Leaders may find themselves paralyzed by analysis, avoiding difficult decisions, or reverting to comfortable but ineffective approaches.

What makes this article particularly valuable is its integration of neuroscience research with practical leadership challenges. Rather than simply identifying problems, Higgins provides a framework for understanding why leadership is inherently difficult from a neurological perspective. This understanding can help leaders develop greater self-awareness and implement strategies to counteract their brain's natural tendencies.

The work emphasizes that successful leadership requires conscious effort to overcome these neurological barriers. Leaders must actively work against their brain's default settings, developing new neural pathways through deliberate practice and mindful attention to all three domains. This process requires both individual commitment and organizational support systems that recognize and address these fundamental challenges.
