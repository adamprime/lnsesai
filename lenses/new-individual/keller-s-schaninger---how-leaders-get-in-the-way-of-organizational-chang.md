# How Leaders Get in the Way of Organizational Change
**Author:** Keller, S., & Schaninger, W.

**Tags:** Thinking Skills / Decision Making, nan

## Summary

"Beyond Performance 2.0: A Proven Approach to Leading Large-Scale Change" by Scott Keller and Bill Schaninger represents a significant evolution from their original work, transforming from a theoretical framework into a practical, actionable guide for organizational transformation. The book addresses one of the most critical challenges facing modern leaders: how to successfully navigate large-scale organizational change in an era of constant disruption and evolving business landscapes.

The central premise of the book revolves around the authors' refined "Five Frames of Performance and Health" approach to change management, which has been extensively tested and validated through McKinsey & Company's research and client work. This framework provides leaders with a comprehensive methodology that addresses both the performance aspects of change (what needs to be accomplished) and the health aspects (how the organization and its people adapt and sustain the transformation).

What distinguishes this work is its evidence-based foundation and practical orientation. Keller and Schaninger present compelling research showing that organizations applying their methodology can more than double their odds of successful transformationâ€”from a typical success rate of merely 30 percent to nearly 80 percent. This dramatic improvement in success rates is achieved through a systematic approach that addresses the common pitfalls and obstacles that derail most change initiatives.

The book's "how-to" format makes it immediately applicable for leaders at all levels. Each chapter provides step-by-step guidance, accompanied by practical tools, templates, and real-world case studies that illustrate how the concepts work in practice. The authors draw from their extensive consulting experience to provide concrete examples of both successful transformations and cautionary tales of failed initiatives.

A key insight throughout the book is that leaders themselves are often the primary obstacle to successful organizational change. The authors identify common leadership behaviors and mindsets that inadvertently sabotage transformation efforts, such as focusing solely on technical aspects while neglecting the human dimension, underestimating the time and resources required for sustainable change, and failing to model the behaviors they expect from others.

The Five Frames approach addresses these leadership challenges by providing a holistic view of transformation that encompasses: aspiration (defining the vision and purpose), architecture (designing the organizational structure and processes), abilities (developing necessary skills and capabilities), atmosphere (creating the right culture and environment), and accountability (establishing governance and measurement systems). This comprehensive framework ensures that leaders consider all critical dimensions of change rather than focusing narrowly on isolated elements.

The book emphasizes the importance of treating organizational health as equally important as performance metrics. While many change initiatives focus exclusively on financial and operational outcomes, Keller and Schaninger argue that sustainable transformation requires building organizational capabilities, employee engagement, and cultural alignment. This dual focus on performance and health creates a foundation for long-term success rather than short-term gains that quickly erode.

Throughout the work, the authors stress the critical role of leadership behavior in modeling and reinforcing change. They provide specific guidance on how leaders can align their actions with their stated intentions, communicate effectively during periods of uncertainty, and build coalitions of support throughout the organization. The book also addresses the emotional and psychological aspects of change, recognizing that transformation is fundamentally a human process that requires careful attention to how people experience and adapt to new ways of working.

The significance of this book lies in its combination of rigorous research, practical applicability, and comprehensive scope. In an era where organizational agility and adaptability are essential for survival, leaders need proven methodologies for managing complex transformations. The authors' approach addresses the full spectrum of change management challenges, from initial strategy formulation through implementation and sustainment.

Moreover, the book's emphasis on doubling success rates addresses a critical business need. With the high cost and disruption associated with large-scale change initiatives, organizations cannot afford repeated failures. By providing a systematic approach backed by evidence and practical experience, Keller and Schaninger offer leaders a roadmap for achieving transformation success while avoiding the common pitfalls that derail most change efforts.

## Theme Analysis

### Theme 1: Evidence-Based Change Management
**Explanation:** The book emphasizes the importance of using data-driven approaches and proven methodologies rather than relying on intuition or popular management fads. The authors ground their recommendations in extensive research and real-world validation.
**Examples:** The documented improvement from 30% to 80% success rates, the rigorous testing of the Five Frames approach across multiple organizations, and the inclusion of specific metrics and measurement tools throughout the methodology.

### Theme 2: Holistic Transformation Approach
**Explanation:** Rather than focusing on isolated aspects of change, the book advocates for a comprehensive approach that addresses all dimensions of organizational transformation simultaneously. This prevents the common problem of optimizing one area while neglecting others.
**Examples:** The Five Frames framework covering aspiration, architecture, abilities, atmosphere, and accountability; the equal emphasis on performance and health metrics; and the integration of technical, cultural, and human elements in change planning.

### Theme 3: Leadership as Change Catalyst and Obstacle
**Explanation:** The book identifies leaders as both the primary drivers of successful change and the most common source of transformation failures. It emphasizes the critical importance of leadership behavior, mindset, and capability in determining outcomes.
**Examples:** Specific guidance on leadership behaviors that support or undermine change efforts, tools for leadership self-assessment and development, and case studies showing how leadership actions directly impact transformation success.

### Theme 4: Practical Implementation Focus
**Explanation:** Unlike purely theoretical works, this book prioritizes actionable guidance and real-world application. Every concept is accompanied by specific tools, templates, and step-by-step processes that leaders can immediately implement.
**Examples:** The transformation from the first edition's theoretical framework to a practical "how-to" guide, inclusion of detailed implementation tools, and extensive use of real-life case studies and examples.

### Theme 5: Sustainable Change vs. Quick Fixes
**Explanation:** The book emphasizes building lasting organizational capabilities and cultural changes rather than pursuing short-term performance improvements that quickly erode. This long-term perspective is essential for creating resilient organizations.
**Examples:** The focus on organizational health alongside performance metrics, emphasis on capability building and skill development, and attention to cultural transformation as a foundation for sustained success.

### Theme 6: Human-Centered Change Management
**Explanation:** The authors recognize that organizational change is fundamentally about people and human behavior. They emphasize the emotional, psychological, and social aspects of transformation alongside technical and structural elements.
**Examples:** Attention to employee engagement and experience during change, guidance on communication and coalition building, and recognition of the human challenges inherent in adapting to new ways of working.
