# Better, Simpler Strategy
**Author:** Oberholzer-Gee, F.

**Tags:** Strategy, nan

## Summary

"Better, Simpler Strategy: A Value-Based Guide to Exceptional Performance" by Felix Oberholzer-Gee presents a revolutionary approach to strategic thinking that cuts through the complexity of traditional strategy frameworks. As a Harvard Business School professor, Oberholzer-Gee argues that most companies overcomplicate their strategic planning, leading to confusion and poor execution. Instead, he proposes a streamlined, value-based approach centered on two fundamental economic forces: customers' willingness to pay (WTP) and suppliers' willingness to sell (WTS).

The book's central thesis revolves around what Oberholzer-Gee calls the "value stick" - a simple yet powerful framework that visualizes value creation. The value stick represents the difference between what customers are willing to pay for a product or service and what suppliers (including employees) are willing to accept for providing their resources. This gap represents the total value created by a firm, which can then be distributed among customers (through lower prices), suppliers (through higher compensation), and shareholders (through profits).

Oberholzer-Gee demonstrates how this framework directly relates to Michael Porter's Five Forces model, but simplifies it into actionable insights. Rather than getting lost in complex competitive analysis, managers can focus on two clear objectives: increasing customers' willingness to pay or decreasing suppliers' willingness to sell. This approach provides a measurable way to evaluate strategic decisions and their impact on value creation.

The book emphasizes that exceptional performance comes from doing less, not more. Oberholzer-Gee shows how successful companies achieve superior results by focusing intensely on activities that either increase WTP or decrease WTS, while eliminating or de-emphasizing everything else. This selective focus allows organizations to allocate resources more effectively and avoid the strategic drift that plagues many businesses.

Throughout the work, Oberholzer-Gee provides practical tools for implementing this value-based approach. He explains how to measure willingness to pay and willingness to sell, how to identify which activities truly drive value creation, and how to make strategic trade-offs using the value stick framework. The methodology offers a clear alternative to complex strategic planning processes that often produce lengthy documents but little actionable direction.

The book addresses common strategic pitfalls, such as pursuing growth for its own sake or copying competitors' strategies without understanding the underlying value dynamics. Instead, Oberholzer-Gee advocates for a more disciplined approach where every strategic initiative is evaluated based on its impact on the value stick. This creates a more coherent and focused strategic direction.

One of the book's key strengths is its practical applicability across different business contexts. Whether dealing with digital transformation, competitive positioning, or operational improvements, the value stick framework provides a consistent lens for evaluation. This universality makes it particularly valuable for managers who need to navigate increasingly complex business environments.

The significance of "Better, Simpler Strategy" lies in its ability to democratize strategic thinking. By reducing strategy to its essential elements, Oberholzer-Gee makes sophisticated strategic analysis accessible to managers at all levels. The framework eliminates much of the jargon and complexity that often surrounds strategic planning, replacing it with clear, measurable concepts that can guide decision-making.

The book also addresses the execution challenge that plagues many strategic initiatives. By providing a simple, memorable framework, it increases the likelihood that strategic insights will actually influence day-to-day decisions throughout the organization. This bridge between strategic thinking and operational execution is crucial for achieving sustained competitive advantage.

Oberholzer-Gee's work represents a significant contribution to strategic management literature by offering a practical alternative to increasingly complex strategic frameworks. It provides leaders with a tool that is both theoretically sound and practically useful, enabling them to cut through strategic complexity and focus on what truly drives value creation and exceptional performance.

## Theme Analysis

### Theme 1: Value Creation Through Simplification
**Explanation:** The book's core argument is that strategic excellence comes from simplifying complex business decisions into clear value-creation activities. Rather than pursuing multiple strategic initiatives simultaneously, companies should focus on fewer activities that directly impact customer willingness to pay or supplier willingness to sell.
**Examples:** Oberholzer-Gee demonstrates how successful companies achieve more by doing less, concentrating their resources on activities that clearly increase the value stick rather than spreading efforts across numerous strategic priorities.

### Theme 2: The Value Stick Framework
**Explanation:** The value stick serves as the book's central analytical tool, measuring value creation as the difference between customer willingness to pay and supplier willingness to sell. This framework provides a quantifiable approach to strategic decision-making that replaces subjective strategic assessments.
**Examples:** The framework directly connects to Porter's Five Forces model but simplifies competitive analysis into two measurable dimensions, making strategic evaluation more accessible and actionable for managers.

### Theme 3: Customer-Centric Value Enhancement
**Explanation:** One pillar of the strategy focuses on increasing customers' willingness to pay through enhanced value propositions, superior products, or improved customer experiences. This represents the demand-side approach to value creation.
**Examples:** The book explores how companies can systematically identify and implement initiatives that make customers more willing to pay premium prices, moving beyond simple cost competition.

### Theme 4: Supplier and Employee Value Optimization
**Explanation:** The second pillar involves decreasing suppliers' willingness to sell, which includes making the company more attractive to employees and suppliers so they accept lower compensation or provide better terms. This represents the supply-side approach to value creation.
**Examples:** Oberholzer-Gee shows how companies can create working environments and partnerships that reduce their cost structure while maintaining or improving quality and service levels.

### Theme 5: Strategic Focus and Resource Allocation
**Explanation:** The book emphasizes the importance of strategic discipline, arguing that companies must eliminate activities that don't contribute to value creation. This selective approach prevents resource dilution and strategic confusion.
**Examples:** The value stick framework provides clear criteria for evaluating which initiatives deserve investment and which should be abandoned, creating more coherent strategic direction.

### Theme 6: Measurable Strategic Performance
**Explanation:** Unlike traditional strategic approaches that rely on qualitative assessments, the value-based strategy provides quantifiable metrics for strategic success. This measurement capability enables more objective strategic evaluation and course correction.
**Examples:** The book demonstrates how willingness to pay and willingness to sell can be measured and tracked over time, providing concrete feedback on strategic effectiveness and enabling data-driven strategic adjustments.
