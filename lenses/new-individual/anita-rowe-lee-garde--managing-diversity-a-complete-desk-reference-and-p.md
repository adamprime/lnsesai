# Managing Diversity: A Complete Desk Reference and Planning Guide
**Author:** Anita Rowe, Lee Gardenswartz

**Tags:** nan, nan

## Summary

"Managing Diversity: A Complete Desk Reference and Planning Guide" by Lee Gardenswartz and Anita Rowe is a comprehensive resource designed to help organizations navigate the rapidly changing demographics of the American workforce. Written in anticipation of the significant demographic shift where, by the year 2000, 70 percent of new workforce entrants would be women and minorities while only 30 percent would be white, American-born males, this book serves as both a theoretical framework and practical toolkit for diversity management.

The authors present diversity not merely as a compliance issue or moral imperative, but as a strategic business advantage that requires systematic planning and implementation. Gardenswartz and Rowe argue that organizations must proactively address diversity challenges to remain competitive and effective in an increasingly multicultural business environment. Their approach emphasizes that managing diversity goes beyond traditional equal employment opportunity measures to encompass a comprehensive transformation of organizational culture, policies, and practices.

The book's framework centers on understanding diversity as multidimensional, encompassing not only visible differences like race and gender, but also invisible dimensions such as work style, communication preferences, values, and life experiences. The authors introduce their "Four Layers of Diversity" model, which categorizes diversity into personality, internal dimensions (age, gender, race, ethnicity, physical ability, and sexual orientation), external dimensions (geographic location, income, personal habits, recreational habits, religion, educational background, work experience, appearance, parental status, and marital status), and organizational dimensions (functional level, work content/field, division/department, seniority, work location, union affiliation, and management status).

A significant portion of the book focuses on practical implementation strategies. The authors provide detailed assessment tools to help organizations evaluate their current diversity climate, identify areas for improvement, and measure progress over time. They emphasize the importance of leadership commitment, arguing that diversity initiatives fail without visible and sustained support from top management. The book outlines step-by-step processes for developing diversity action plans, including how to form diversity task forces, conduct organizational assessments, and create accountability systems.

The authors address common barriers to diversity implementation, including resistance to change, unconscious bias, and systemic organizational obstacles. They provide strategies for overcoming these challenges through education, policy changes, and cultural transformation initiatives. The book emphasizes the importance of creating inclusive environments where all employees can contribute their full potential, rather than simply increasing numerical representation of different groups.

Communication emerges as a critical theme throughout the book, with the authors providing guidance on cross-cultural communication, conflict resolution, and building bridges across different groups. They recognize that diversity can create both opportunities and tensions, and they offer practical tools for managing these dynamics constructively.

The book also addresses the business case for diversity, presenting research and examples demonstrating how diverse organizations outperform homogeneous ones in creativity, problem-solving, market understanding, and financial performance. The authors argue that diversity is not just socially responsible but economically advantageous, particularly as markets become increasingly global and diverse.

At 446 pages, this comprehensive desk reference includes numerous worksheets, assessment tools, checklists, and planning templates that organizations can immediately implement. The practical nature of these resources makes the book valuable not only for understanding diversity concepts but for taking concrete action.

The significance of this work lies in its timing and comprehensive approach. Published during a period of rapid demographic change in the American workforce, it provided organizations with both the rationale and the tools needed to adapt successfully. The book's enduring relevance stems from its systematic approach to diversity management and its recognition that diversity work requires ongoing commitment and strategic planning rather than one-time initiatives. By treating diversity as a complex organizational change process, Gardenswartz and Rowe helped establish diversity management as a legitimate business discipline requiring specialized knowledge and skills.

## Theme Analysis

### Theme 1: Demographic Transformation and Workforce Evolution
**Explanation:** The book emphasizes the fundamental demographic shift occurring in the American workforce, where traditional majority groups are becoming minorities among new entrants. This transformation requires organizations to completely rethink their approaches to recruitment, retention, and management practices.
**Examples:** The authors highlight the statistic that 70% of new workforce entrants will be women and minorities, and they provide case studies showing how organizations that adapt early gain competitive advantages in talent acquisition and market reach.

### Theme 2: Multidimensional Nature of Diversity
**Explanation:** Rather than viewing diversity through a narrow lens of race and gender, the authors present a comprehensive model that recognizes multiple layers of human difference. This broader perspective helps organizations address the full spectrum of diversity challenges and opportunities.
**Examples:** The Four Layers of Diversity model includes personality, internal dimensions, external dimensions, and organizational dimensions, with specific examples of how each layer impacts workplace dynamics and requires different management approaches.

### Theme 3: Strategic Business Imperative
**Explanation:** The book positions diversity management as a critical business strategy rather than merely a compliance requirement or social responsibility initiative. This approach emphasizes the competitive advantages and financial benefits of effective diversity management.
**Examples:** The authors present research linking diverse teams to improved innovation, better decision-making, and enhanced market understanding, along with case studies of organizations that achieved measurable business results through diversity initiatives.

### Theme 4: Systematic Implementation and Planning
**Explanation:** Effective diversity management requires structured, systematic approaches rather than ad hoc efforts. The book emphasizes the importance of assessment, planning, implementation, and evaluation in creating sustainable diversity initiatives.
**Examples:** The authors provide detailed planning templates, assessment tools, and step-by-step implementation guides, including how to form diversity councils, conduct climate surveys, and establish accountability measures.

### Theme 5: Leadership Commitment and Cultural Change
**Explanation:** Successful diversity initiatives require visible, sustained commitment from organizational leadership and fundamental changes to organizational culture. Without this foundation, diversity efforts typically fail to achieve meaningful results.
**Examples:** The book includes examples of leadership behaviors that support diversity, methods for engaging senior executives, and strategies for embedding diversity values into organizational culture and decision-making processes.

### Theme 6: Inclusive Environment Creation
**Explanation:** Beyond increasing numerical representation, organizations must create inclusive environments where all employees feel valued and can contribute their full potential. This requires addressing both overt and subtle barriers to inclusion.
**Examples:** The authors provide guidance on identifying and eliminating systemic barriers, creating mentoring programs, developing inclusive communication practices, and building cross-cultural competence among managers and employees.
