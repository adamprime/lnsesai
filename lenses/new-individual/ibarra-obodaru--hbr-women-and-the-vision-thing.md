# HBR Women and the Vision Thing
**Author:** Ibarra & Obodaru

**Tags:** Communication, Intercultural Communication

## Summary

"Women and the Vision Thing" by Herminia Ibarra and Otilia Obodaru addresses a critical challenge facing women in leadership: the persistent perception that they lack "vision" - the ability to see opportunities, craft broad strategic perspectives, and inspire others toward ambitious goals. This Harvard Business Review article examines why women continue to be underrepresented in top executive positions despite making significant strides in middle management roles.

The authors' research reveals a complex paradox: while women have achieved near-parity in many professional fields and demonstrate strong performance in operational and functional roles, they consistently receive lower ratings on visionary leadership capabilities during executive evaluations. Through interviews with female executives and analysis of evaluation data, Ibarra and Obodaru explore whether this "vision deficit" represents an actual skills gap or a perceptual bias rooted in organizational and societal expectations.

The research uncovers that the issue is multifaceted, involving both structural barriers and behavioral patterns. Women often excel in execution-focused roles that emphasize detailed planning, risk management, and incremental improvement - competencies that are highly valued but may not be perceived as "visionary." Meanwhile, the traditional conception of visionary leadership often aligns with stereotypically masculine traits: bold risk-taking, charismatic communication, and transformational thinking that challenges the status quo.

The authors identify several key factors contributing to this perception gap. First, women frequently receive assignments that are more operational in nature, limiting their exposure to strategic, enterprise-wide challenges that would allow them to develop and demonstrate visionary capabilities. Second, when women do articulate bold visions, they may face different reception than their male counterparts, with their ideas being questioned more rigorously or attributed to other sources.

The article also explores how women's communication styles may inadvertently contribute to perceptions of limited vision. Women often present ideas with more nuance, acknowledging potential obstacles and alternative perspectives - an approach that demonstrates thoughtful analysis but may be interpreted as lack of conviction or clarity of vision. In contrast, the confident, simplified messaging often associated with visionary leadership may feel inauthentic or inappropriate to many women leaders.

Ibarra and Obodaru emphasize that addressing this challenge requires both individual and organizational interventions. For individual women, the authors suggest developing comfort with ambiguity, learning to communicate vision with greater conviction, and actively seeking stretch assignments that require strategic thinking. They also recommend finding ways to articulate a compelling future state while maintaining authenticity in leadership style.

At the organizational level, the authors call for more inclusive definitions of visionary leadership that recognize different styles of strategic thinking and communication. Companies need to examine their evaluation criteria and ensure that assessments of vision don't inadvertently favor particular communication styles or personality types. Additionally, organizations should be more intentional about providing women with opportunities to lead transformational initiatives and gain experience in enterprise-wide strategic roles.

The significance of this work extends beyond individual career advancement to broader questions of organizational effectiveness and innovation. The authors argue that diverse perspectives in visionary leadership can enhance strategic decision-making and organizational adaptability. When organizations fail to recognize or develop visionary capabilities in women, they potentially limit their access to valuable strategic insights and leadership approaches.

The article challenges both women and organizations to reconsider assumptions about what visionary leadership looks like and how it can be developed and recognized. Rather than simply encouraging women to adopt traditionally masculine approaches to vision communication, the authors advocate for expanding organizational understanding of how strategic insight and inspirational leadership can be expressed through different styles and approaches.

## Theme Analysis

### Theme 1: Perception vs. Reality in Leadership Assessment
**Explanation:** The article explores whether women's perceived lack of vision represents actual capability gaps or biased evaluation processes. This theme examines how organizational assessment systems may inadvertently favor certain leadership styles over others.
**Examples:** The research shows women receiving lower vision ratings despite strong operational performance, and the tendency for evaluators to interpret women's nuanced communication as lack of strategic clarity.

### Theme 2: Gendered Communication Styles and Vision Articulation
**Explanation:** Women's tendency toward more collaborative, nuanced communication may be misinterpreted as lacking conviction or strategic clarity. The theme explores how different communication approaches affect perception of visionary capability.
**Examples:** Women presenting ideas with acknowledgment of risks and alternatives being seen as less decisive, while simplified, confident messaging is more readily recognized as visionary leadership.

### Theme 3: Structural Barriers and Assignment Patterns
**Explanation:** Organizational systems often channel women into operational roles that limit opportunities to develop and demonstrate strategic vision. This creates a cycle where lack of exposure leads to perceived lack of capability.
**Examples:** Women receiving more execution-focused assignments rather than transformational leadership opportunities, limiting their ability to gain experience in enterprise-wide strategic thinking.

### Theme 4: Redefining Visionary Leadership
**Explanation:** The article challenges traditional, often masculine-coded definitions of visionary leadership and advocates for more inclusive models. This involves recognizing diverse approaches to strategic thinking and inspiration.
**Examples:** Expanding recognition beyond charismatic, risk-taking leadership styles to include collaborative vision-building and analytical strategic approaches that women often demonstrate.

### Theme 5: Individual and Organizational Development Strategies
**Explanation:** Addressing the vision gap requires coordinated efforts at both personal skill development and systemic organizational change. Neither individual adaptation nor structural reform alone is sufficient.
**Examples:** Women learning to communicate vision with greater conviction while organizations examine evaluation criteria and provide more diverse strategic leadership opportunities.

### Theme 6: Strategic Value of Leadership Diversity
**Explanation:** The article argues that diverse approaches to visionary leadership enhance organizational strategic capability and innovation. Limiting vision recognition to narrow styles reduces organizational effectiveness.
**Examples:** Different perspectives in strategic thinking contributing to better decision-making and the potential loss of valuable insights when women's visionary capabilities are underrecognized or underdeveloped.
