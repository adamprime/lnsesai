# Will Your Competitive Advantage Work in Other Markets?
**Author:** Poojary, A.

**Tags:** Strategy, Thinking Skills / Decision Making

## Summary

"Will Your Competitive Advantage Work in Other Markets?" by A. Poojary addresses one of the most critical challenges facing multinational companies: the assumption that competitive advantages developed in home markets will automatically translate to success in international markets. Based on extensive research involving over 100 in-depth interviews with executives at multinational companies, Poojary reveals why this assumption is often flawed and provides a framework for navigating international expansion more effectively.

The book's central premise challenges the conventional wisdom that successful business strategies are universally applicable. Poojary demonstrates that competitive advantages are often context-dependent, shaped by specific market conditions, customer behaviors, and competitive dynamics that may not exist in other markets. This insight is particularly relevant in today's globalized economy, where companies increasingly seek growth through international expansion but often struggle to replicate their domestic success abroad.

Through rigorous analysis of real-world cases, Poojary identifies three primary hurdles that companies encounter when attempting to transfer their competitive advantages to new markets. First, differences in the local competitive landscape can render previously effective strategies obsolete. What works against competitors in one market may be insufficient or inappropriate when facing different competitive dynamics, market structures, or regulatory environments in another market. Second, variations in local customer preferences can undermine value propositions that were highly successful elsewhere. Customer needs, purchasing behaviors, cultural values, and expectations vary significantly across markets, making it essential to understand and adapt to local preferences. Third, companies often face limited willingness or ability to adapt their strategies to meet local demands, whether due to organizational inertia, resource constraints, or strategic inflexibility.

The book's key contribution lies in its practical framework for addressing these challenges. Rather than simply identifying problems, Poojary provides actionable solutions through three strategic approaches. The first approach involves making small adjustments to existing competitive advantages, fine-tuning strategies to better align with local market conditions while maintaining core strategic elements. This approach is most suitable when the fundamental competitive advantage remains relevant but requires minor modifications to achieve effectiveness in the new market.

The second approach is more radical: developing entirely new competitive advantages specifically tailored to the target market. This strategy acknowledges that sometimes the gap between home and target markets is too significant for minor adjustments, requiring companies to build new capabilities, value propositions, or strategic positions. While more resource-intensive, this approach can lead to sustainable competitive advantages that are specifically designed for local market conditions.

The third approach, though often overlooked, is equally important: the strategic decision to leave the market entirely. Poojary argues that companies should be willing to acknowledge when their competitive advantages cannot be effectively adapted or when the cost of developing new advantages exceeds potential returns. This strategic discipline prevents companies from pursuing unprofitable international ventures and allows them to focus resources on markets where they can achieve sustainable competitive advantage.

The book emphasizes the importance of systematic analysis before international expansion. Companies must thoroughly assess whether their competitive advantages are transferable, understand local market dynamics, and honestly evaluate their capacity for adaptation. This analytical approach helps prevent costly mistakes and increases the likelihood of successful international expansion.

Poojary's work is particularly significant because it addresses a gap between strategic theory and international business practice. While much strategic literature focuses on developing competitive advantages, less attention has been paid to the challenges of transferring these advantages across markets. The book provides a bridge between competitive strategy and international business, offering practical insights for executives navigating global expansion.

The research methodology, based on extensive interviews with multinational executives, lends credibility to the findings and ensures that the insights reflect real-world challenges rather than theoretical abstractions. This empirical foundation makes the book particularly valuable for practitioners who need actionable guidance for international expansion decisions.

## Theme Analysis

### Theme 1: Context-Dependent Nature of Competitive Advantage
**Explanation:** Competitive advantages are not universal assets but are deeply embedded in specific market contexts. What creates value in one market may be irrelevant or even counterproductive in another due to different competitive dynamics, customer needs, and market structures.
**Examples:** The book illustrates how companies that dominate their home markets through specific strategies often struggle internationally when those same strategies fail to resonate with local customers or compete effectively against different types of competitors.

### Theme 2: Market-Specific Adaptation Requirements
**Explanation:** Successful international expansion requires companies to understand and adapt to local market conditions rather than simply replicating home market strategies. This adaptation can range from minor adjustments to complete strategic overhauls.
**Examples:** Through executive interviews, Poojary shows how companies must modify their value propositions, competitive positioning, and strategic approaches to align with local customer preferences and competitive landscapes.

### Theme 3: Strategic Decision-Making Framework
**Explanation:** The book provides a systematic approach to evaluating international expansion opportunities through three strategic options: minor adjustments, developing new advantages, or market exit. This framework helps companies make informed decisions about international expansion.
**Examples:** Poojary presents cases where companies successfully applied each of the three approaches, demonstrating when each strategy is most appropriate based on market conditions and company capabilities.

### Theme 4: Organizational Learning and Flexibility
**Explanation:** Companies must develop organizational capabilities for learning about new markets and adapting their strategies accordingly. Rigid adherence to home market strategies often leads to international failure.
**Examples:** The research reveals how successful multinational companies invest in understanding local markets and develop organizational flexibility to modify their competitive approaches based on local insights.

### Theme 5: Risk Assessment and Strategic Discipline
**Explanation:** International expansion requires honest assessment of risks and the discipline to exit markets where competitive advantages cannot be effectively established or maintained. Not all markets are worth entering or staying in.
**Examples:** The book highlights cases where companies made difficult but strategically sound decisions to exit markets where their competitive advantages could not be successfully adapted or developed.

### Theme 6: Integration of Competitive Strategy and International Business
**Explanation:** The book bridges the gap between competitive strategy theory and international business practice, showing how strategic concepts must be adapted for global application rather than applied universally.
**Examples:** Poojary demonstrates how traditional competitive strategy frameworks need modification when applied across different markets, requiring integration with international business considerations such as cultural differences and local competitive dynamics.
