# Performance Management Shouldn't Kill Collaboration
**Author:** Cross, R., & Grant, A.

**Tags:** Organizational Development / HR Theory, Performance Management

## Summary

"Performance Management Shouldn't Kill Collaboration" by Rob Cross and Adam Grant addresses a critical paradox in modern organizations: while businesses increasingly depend on cross-functional collaboration to solve complex problems and drive innovation, traditional performance management systems often undermine the very collaborative behaviors they need to succeed.

**Overview**

The authors' research involving more than 8,000 senior managers across diverse sectors including biotech, banking, consumer products, energy, and law reveals that siloed approaches to performance targets create significant barriers to collaboration. The fundamental problem lies in how organizations structure goals and incentives—when employees are evaluated and rewarded solely on individual metrics within their functional areas, they have little motivation to invest time and energy in collaborative efforts that benefit the broader organization but may not directly impact their personal performance ratings.

Cross and Grant argue that this misalignment between performance systems and collaborative needs is not just inefficient—it's counterproductive in today's interconnected business environment. Organizations face increasingly complex challenges that require expertise from multiple departments, yet their performance management systems continue to operate as if work happens in isolation. This creates a tension where employees must choose between optimizing their individual performance metrics and contributing to collaborative initiatives that could deliver greater organizational value.

**Key Concepts**

The centerpiece of the authors' solution is a comprehensive four-part performance scorecard designed to balance individual accountability with collaborative imperatives. This framework recognizes that effective performance management must operate at multiple levels simultaneously.

The first component, cross-silo goals, establishes broad shared objectives that focus on significant organizational challenges achievable within a year, such as cutting time-to-market for new products by half. These goals require coordination across departments and ensure that collaboration becomes a measurable part of everyone's job rather than an optional extra activity.

Team goals form the second element, measuring team-level results and holding individuals accountable for elevating their entire working group's performance. This creates incentives for knowledge sharing, mutual support, and collective problem-solving within immediate work units.

Individual goals comprise the third component, measuring each person's specific contribution to higher-level objectives while maintaining personal accountability. This ensures that the focus on collaboration doesn't eliminate individual responsibility or allow poor performers to hide within group achievements.

The fourth element, though not fully detailed in the available sources, likely addresses behavioral or developmental aspects that support both individual excellence and collaborative effectiveness.

**Significance**

This work matters because it addresses a fundamental design flaw in how most organizations manage performance. As businesses become more complex and interdependent, the ability to collaborate effectively across boundaries becomes a competitive advantage. However, many companies inadvertently sabotage their collaborative potential through performance systems that were designed for simpler, more hierarchical organizational structures.

The research base of over 8,000 senior managers provides substantial evidence that this isn't merely a theoretical problem—it's a widespread organizational challenge affecting multiple industries. The authors' framework offers a practical solution that doesn't abandon individual accountability but rather embeds it within a broader system that recognizes the reality of how work actually gets done in modern organizations.

The book's significance extends beyond performance management to organizational design more broadly. It highlights how seemingly technical HR processes can have profound impacts on organizational culture, innovation capacity, and competitive performance. By showing how to align performance systems with collaborative needs, Cross and Grant provide a roadmap for organizations seeking to harness the full potential of their human capital.

The timing of this work is particularly relevant as organizations grapple with remote work, matrix structures, and increasingly complex customer demands that require seamless coordination across traditional organizational boundaries. The four-part scorecard offers a concrete tool for leaders who recognize the need for collaboration but struggle with how to make it happen systematically rather than relying on individual goodwill or informal networks.

## Theme Analysis

### Theme 1: Misalignment Between Performance Systems and Organizational Needs
**Explanation:** Traditional performance management creates a fundamental disconnect between how organizations need people to work (collaboratively across silos) and how they measure and reward performance (individually within functions). This misalignment undermines organizational effectiveness by discouraging the very behaviors that drive success in complex, interconnected business environments.
**Examples:** The research across 8,000+ managers demonstrates how siloed performance targets consistently emerge as barriers to collaboration across biotech, banking, consumer products, energy, and legal sectors.

### Theme 2: Multi-Level Performance Architecture
**Explanation:** Effective performance management must operate simultaneously at individual, team, and cross-organizational levels rather than focusing exclusively on individual metrics. This multi-level approach ensures that collaborative behaviors are systematically encouraged and measured while maintaining personal accountability.
**Examples:** The four-part scorecard includes cross-silo goals for broad organizational challenges, team goals for group performance, and individual goals for personal contributions, creating a comprehensive performance architecture.

### Theme 3: Systematic Collaboration Design
**Explanation:** Rather than leaving collaboration to chance or individual initiative, organizations must deliberately design systems that make collaborative behavior a measurable and rewarded part of everyone's role. This transforms collaboration from an optional activity into a core job requirement.
**Examples:** Cross-silo goals like "cutting time-to-market by half" require systematic coordination across departments and make collaboration a concrete, measurable objective rather than an abstract aspiration.

### Theme 4: Accountability Without Isolation
**Explanation:** The framework demonstrates that organizations don't need to choose between individual accountability and collaborative effectiveness—they can design systems that achieve both simultaneously. This challenges the false dichotomy that often paralyzes performance management discussions.
**Examples:** Individual goals within the scorecard maintain personal accountability while being nested within team and cross-silo objectives that require collaborative achievement.

### Theme 5: Evidence-Based Organizational Design
**Explanation:** The authors ground their recommendations in substantial empirical research rather than theoretical speculation, providing organizations with confidence that these approaches address real, widespread challenges rather than isolated problems.
**Examples:** The research base of over 8,000 senior managers across multiple industries provides robust evidence that siloed performance approaches consistently undermine collaboration across diverse organizational contexts.
