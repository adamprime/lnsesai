# Journal of Business and Technical Communication, Vol 13 No. 3 July 1999 "And Then She Said"
**Author:** Jeanne W. Herrick

**Tags:** Communication, Intercultural Communication

## Summary

"And Then She Said" by Jeanne W. Herrick, published in the Journal of Business and Technical Communication in July 1999, presents a compelling argument for adopting a rhetorical perspective when examining the complex intersections of gender, communication, and power dynamics in workplace settings. Herrick's work stands as a significant contribution to organizational communication scholarship by demonstrating how narrative analysis can reveal the subtle yet pervasive ways gender influences professional interactions and power structures.

The article employs a dual approach to narrative analysis, using stories both as data collection tools and as analytical frameworks for understanding workplace dynamics. Herrick argues that traditional approaches to studying gender and communication in professional contexts often fail to capture the nuanced, contextual nature of how power operates through everyday communicative practices. By focusing on narratives—the stories people tell about their workplace experiences—she reveals how gender-based power differentials are constructed, maintained, and sometimes challenged through communication.

Central to Herrick's argument is the recognition that workplace communication is never neutral or purely functional. Instead, she demonstrates how communicative acts are inherently rhetorical, serving to position individuals within existing power structures while simultaneously offering opportunities for resistance and change. The narratives she analyzes reveal patterns of gendered communication that often disadvantage women in professional settings, from interruption patterns in meetings to the ways women's contributions are acknowledged or dismissed.

Herrick's rhetorical perspective emphasizes the importance of context in understanding how gender and power intersect in workplace communication. Rather than viewing gender as a fixed category that determines communication styles, she presents it as a dynamic social construction that emerges through interaction. This approach allows for a more sophisticated understanding of how individuals navigate gendered expectations while pursuing professional goals, and how organizational cultures both reflect and shape broader societal attitudes about gender and authority.

The article's methodological contribution lies in its demonstration of how narrative analysis can uncover the often-invisible ways power operates through communication. By examining the stories people tell about their workplace experiences, Herrick reveals the gap between official organizational policies promoting equality and the lived realities of gendered communication practices. These narratives serve as windows into the informal power structures that often contradict formal organizational hierarchies.

Herrick's work also highlights the performative nature of gender in professional contexts, showing how individuals must constantly negotiate between professional competence and gendered expectations. The narratives reveal the double binds many women face in workplace communication—being perceived as too aggressive when assertive or too weak when collaborative. This analysis contributes to understanding why formal equality policies often fail to translate into substantive change in organizational dynamics.

The significance of this work extends beyond academic discourse to practical implications for organizational development and communication training. By revealing how power operates through seemingly mundane communicative practices, Herrick's analysis provides insights for creating more equitable workplace environments. Her rhetorical approach suggests that meaningful change requires attention not just to formal policies but to the everyday communicative practices that constitute organizational culture.

Furthermore, the article contributes to broader theoretical discussions about the relationship between discourse and power, drawing on feminist rhetorical theory to illuminate how gender shapes professional communication. Herrick's work demonstrates the value of interdisciplinary approaches that combine rhetorical analysis with organizational communication research, offering tools for understanding how social identities intersect with professional roles.

The article's enduring relevance lies in its recognition that workplace equality cannot be achieved simply through policy changes but requires fundamental shifts in communicative practices and cultural assumptions. By providing a framework for analyzing how power operates through communication, Herrick offers both scholars and practitioners tools for identifying and addressing subtle forms of gender-based discrimination that persist in contemporary workplaces.

## Theme Analysis

### Theme 1: Rhetorical Nature of Workplace Communication
**Explanation:** Herrick argues that all workplace communication is inherently rhetorical, serving to construct and maintain power relationships rather than simply conveying information. This perspective reveals how seemingly neutral professional interactions actually reinforce or challenge existing hierarchies.
**Examples:** The analysis of meeting dynamics where women's contributions are reframed or attributed to male colleagues, and how the framing of ideas differs based on the gender of the speaker.

### Theme 2: Narrative as Analytical Tool
**Explanation:** The article demonstrates how personal narratives serve as both data sources and analytical frameworks for understanding workplace dynamics. Stories reveal the gap between official organizational policies and lived experiences of gendered communication.
**Examples:** Employee stories about being interrupted, having ideas dismissed, or facing different expectations based on gender provide evidence of systemic communication patterns that formal policies fail to address.

### Theme 3: Gender as Performance in Professional Contexts
**Explanation:** Rather than treating gender as a fixed characteristic, Herrick shows how it is continuously performed and negotiated through workplace interactions. This performative aspect creates ongoing challenges for professional identity construction.
**Examples:** The double binds women face when choosing communication styles—being labeled as too aggressive when assertive or too passive when collaborative—illustrate how gender performance constrains professional options.

### Theme 4: Power Dynamics Through Communication Patterns
**Explanation:** The article reveals how power operates through subtle communicative practices that often go unnoticed but significantly impact workplace relationships and career advancement. These patterns maintain existing hierarchies while appearing natural or merit-based.
**Examples:** Analysis of interruption patterns, attribution of ideas, and different standards for evaluating the same communicative behaviors when performed by men versus women.

### Theme 5: Context-Dependent Nature of Gendered Communication
**Explanation:** Herrick emphasizes that gender's impact on workplace communication varies significantly based on organizational culture, industry norms, and specific situational factors. This contextual approach avoids essentialist assumptions about gender differences.
**Examples:** How the same communication style may be received differently in different organizational contexts, and how women's strategies for navigating gendered expectations vary based on their position and organizational culture.

### Theme 6: Resistance and Agency Within Constraints
**Explanation:** While highlighting systemic constraints, the article also examines how individuals find ways to resist and challenge gendered communication norms. This theme emphasizes human agency within structural limitations.
**Examples:** Stories of women who successfully navigate or challenge gendered expectations, and instances where organizational cultures begin to shift through individual and collective resistance to traditional communication patterns.
