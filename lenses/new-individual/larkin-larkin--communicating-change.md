# Communicating Change
**Author:** Larkin & Larkin

**Tags:** Communication, Managing Change

## Summary

"Communicating Change: Winning Employee Support for New Business Goals" by T.J. Larkin and Sandar Larkin challenges conventional wisdom about organizational communication during periods of change. Based on five decades of research, the authors argue that traditional top-down communication strategies are fundamentally flawed and offer a revolutionary approach to engaging employees during transformation initiatives.

The central thesis of the book contradicts the popular belief that senior executives should be the primary communicators of change. Instead, the Larkins demonstrate that front-line supervisors—those closest to the workforce—are the most credible and effective messengers for change initiatives. This finding emerged from extensive research analyzing communication successes and failures across major multinational corporations including General Motors, Polaroid, Xerox, Hewlett-Packard, British Telecom, GE, and IBM.

The authors present three core principles that form the foundation of effective change communication. First, they emphasize that employees trust their immediate supervisors more than senior management when it comes to information about how changes will affect their daily work lives. This trust stems from the supervisor's proximity to front-line operations and their understanding of practical workplace realities. Second, the book argues that communication must be face-to-face rather than relying on impersonal channels like newsletters, videos, or large group meetings. Personal interaction allows for immediate feedback, clarification of concerns, and demonstrates genuine commitment to the change process. Third, the authors stress that communication should focus on job-related information rather than broad organizational vision statements, as employees are primarily concerned with how changes will impact their specific roles and responsibilities.

The Larkins provide extensive evidence showing why traditional communication approaches fail. They demonstrate that employees often view messages from senior executives with skepticism, particularly when those messages seem disconnected from operational realities. The research reveals that workers consistently rate their immediate supervisors as more credible sources of information about workplace changes than company newsletters, videos, or presentations from top management. This credibility gap becomes particularly pronounced during times of uncertainty when employees are seeking honest, practical information about their future.

The book offers practical frameworks for implementing supervisor-based communication strategies. The authors provide detailed guidance on training supervisors to become effective change communicators, including how to prepare them with accurate information, coach them on delivery techniques, and support them throughout the change process. They emphasize that supervisors need both the information and the communication skills to effectively engage their teams, as well as ongoing support from management to maintain credibility.

Drawing from real-world case studies, the authors illustrate both successful and failed change communication efforts. These examples demonstrate the tangible business impact of communication choices, showing how effective supervisor-based communication can accelerate change adoption while poor communication strategies can derail even well-planned initiatives. The case studies span various industries and change scenarios, from technological implementations to organizational restructuring, providing readers with diverse examples of principles in action.

The significance of "Communicating Change" lies in its evidence-based challenge to established communication practices. At a time when organizations frequently struggle with change implementation, the book offers a research-backed alternative to conventional approaches. The authors' emphasis on supervisor credibility and face-to-face communication provides practical solutions for managers seeking to improve employee engagement during transformation efforts.

The book's impact extends beyond simple communication tactics to address fundamental questions about organizational trust and influence. By highlighting the critical role of front-line supervisors, the Larkins advocate for a more distributed approach to leadership during change, recognizing that effective transformation requires engagement at all organizational levels rather than relying solely on executive mandate.

Written for busy managers at all organizational levels, the book combines academic rigor with practical applicability. The authors translate decades of research into actionable strategies that can be immediately implemented, making it valuable for both communication professionals and general managers responsible for leading change initiatives. The work remains relevant as organizations continue to grapple with rapid change and the ongoing challenge of maintaining employee engagement during periods of uncertainty and transformation.

## Theme Analysis

### Theme 1: Supervisor Credibility Over Executive Authority
**Explanation:** The book's central theme argues that front-line supervisors possess greater credibility with employees than senior executives when communicating change. This credibility stems from supervisors' proximity to daily operations and their understanding of practical workplace realities.
**Examples:** Research data showing employees consistently rate immediate supervisors as more trustworthy sources of change information than company newsletters or executive presentations. Case studies from companies like GM and IBM demonstrate how supervisor-led communication achieved better employee buy-in than traditional top-down approaches.

### Theme 2: Face-to-Face Communication Effectiveness
**Explanation:** The authors emphasize that personal, direct communication significantly outperforms impersonal channels like videos, newsletters, or large group meetings. Face-to-face interaction allows for immediate feedback, clarification, and demonstrates genuine commitment to addressing employee concerns.
**Examples:** Comparative analysis of communication methods across multinational corporations showing higher engagement rates with supervisor-led meetings versus mass communication tools. Examples from Hewlett-Packard and Xerox illustrating how personal interaction improved change acceptance rates.

### Theme 3: Job-Focused Information Over Vision Statements
**Explanation:** Employees respond better to specific, job-related information about how changes will affect their daily work rather than broad organizational vision statements or abstract strategic goals. Practical concerns take precedence over inspirational messaging.
**Examples:** Research findings showing employee preference for concrete information about role changes, job security, and workflow modifications. Case studies demonstrating how companies achieved better results when focusing on operational impacts rather than corporate vision during change initiatives.

### Theme 4: Evidence-Based Communication Strategy
**Explanation:** The book challenges conventional wisdom through rigorous research spanning five decades, using empirical evidence to support its recommendations rather than relying on popular management theories or intuitive approaches.
**Examples:** Systematic analysis of communication successes and failures across major corporations including British Telecom and Polaroid. Statistical evidence showing correlation between supervisor-based communication and successful change implementation rates.

### Theme 5: Practical Implementation Framework
**Explanation:** Beyond theoretical insights, the authors provide concrete guidance for training supervisors, preparing communication materials, and supporting front-line managers throughout the change process. The focus is on actionable strategies rather than abstract concepts.
**Examples:** Detailed protocols for supervisor training programs, templates for communication preparation, and step-by-step guides for implementing face-to-face communication strategies. Real-world implementation examples from various industries and organizational contexts.

### Theme 6: Trust and Organizational Influence
**Explanation:** The book addresses fundamental questions about where trust and influence actually reside within organizations, challenging assumptions about hierarchical authority and demonstrating that proximity often trumps position in terms of credible communication.
**Examples:** Analysis of trust patterns across organizational levels showing stronger employee connections to immediate supervisors. Case studies illustrating how companies leveraged existing trust relationships to facilitate change rather than relying solely on positional authority.
