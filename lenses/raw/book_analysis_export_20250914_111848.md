Problems arise when organizations try to apply the wrong coordinating mechanism to their situation or when they attempt to combine incompatible elements from different forms without understanding the underlying tensions. # Theory in Practice: Increasing professional Effectiveness
**Author:** Argyris, Schone

**Tags:** Managing, OD

## Summary

"Theory in Practice: Increasing Professional Effectiveness" by Chris Argyris and Donald Schön is a groundbreaking work that explores the gap between what professionals say they do (espoused theory) and what they actually do (theory-in-use). Published in 1974, this seminal book introduces revolutionary concepts about organizational learning, professional development, and the barriers that prevent individuals and organizations from achieving genuine effectiveness.

The central premise of the book is that most professionals operate under two distinct theories of action. Their espoused theory represents their stated beliefs, values, and intended behaviors - what they claim guides their actions. However, their theory-in-use represents the actual theories that govern their behavior, which can be inferred from observing their actions rather than listening to their words. This disconnect creates significant problems in professional effectiveness and organizational learning.

Argyris and Schön identify two primary models of theory-in-use that dominate professional behavior. Model I is characterized by the desire to remain in unilateral control, maximize winning while minimizing losing, suppress negative feelings, and behave according to what is considered rational. While these behaviors may seem logical, they actually create defensive routines, reduce learning, and decrease long-term effectiveness. Most professionals operate under Model I without realizing it, believing they are being helpful and effective while actually creating counterproductive dynamics.

Model II represents an alternative approach focused on valid information, free and informed choice, and internal commitment to decisions. This model encourages inquiry, reduces defensiveness, and promotes genuine learning. However, transitioning from Model I to Model II requires significant personal and organizational change, as it challenges deeply held assumptions about effectiveness and control.

The authors introduce the crucial distinction between single-loop and double-loop learning. Single-loop learning occurs when errors are detected and corrected without questioning the underlying assumptions, policies, or objectives. It's like a thermostat that maintains temperature without questioning whether the current setting is appropriate. Double-loop learning, however, involves questioning the fundamental assumptions and values that guide behavior. It asks not just "How can we do this better?" but "Should we be doing this at all?"

The book demonstrates how defensive routines - systematic ways of dealing with threat or embarrassment - prevent double-loop learning and maintain the status quo. These routines are often undiscussable, and their undiscussability is also undiscussable, creating what the authors call "organizational defensive loops." These patterns become self-sealing, making it extremely difficult for individuals and organizations to recognize and change counterproductive behaviors.

Unlike charismatic celebrity CEOs, Level 5 leaders focus on building enduring greatness that extends beyond their tenure.

Collins introduces the concept of getting the "right people on the bus" before determining where to drive it. Great companies prioritize hiring and retaining the right people, then figure out the best direction. This people-first philosophy ensures that organizations have the human capital necessary to adapt and thrive regardless of changing circumstances.

The Hedgehog Concept represents another crucial framework, based on Isaiah Berlin's essay about the hedgehog that knows "one big thing" versus the fox that knows many things. Great companies identify the intersection of three critical circles: what they can be the best in the world at, what drives their economic engine, and what they are deeply passionate about. This focused approach allows them to excel in their chosen domain rather than diversifying into areas where they cannot achieve superiority.

Collins also identifies the importance of confronting brutal facts while maintaining unwavering faith in ultimate success—what he calls the Stockdale Paradox, named after Admiral James Stockdale's approach to surviving as a prisoner of war. Great companies create a culture where truth is heard and difficult realities are addressed head-on, yet they never lose faith in their ability to prevail.

The concept of a Culture of Discipline emphasizes that great companies combine entrepreneurial spirit with disciplined action. Technology serves as an accelerator rather than a creator of momentum in great companies. Collins found that good-to-great companies didn't necessarily pioneer new technologies but thoughtfully selected and applied technologies that directly supported their Hedgehog Concept. They avoided technology fads and instead focused on technologies that could accelerate their flywheel effect.

The Flywheel Effect describes how great transformations happen through consistent effort in a single direction rather than dramatic breakthroughs. Like pushing a heavy flywheel, initial efforts may seem to produce little movement, but persistent pushing in a consistent direction eventually creates unstoppable momentum.

The book's significance lies in its empirical approach to understanding corporate excellence. Rather than relying on popular business theories or charismatic case studies, Collins used rigorous research methodology to identify patterns among companies that achieved sustained superior performance. The findings challenged conventional wisdom about leadership, strategy, and organizational change.

"Good to Great" has influenced countless business leaders and organizations by providing a practical framework for achieving and sustaining excellence. Its principles have been applied across various industries and sectors, from Fortune 500 companies to non-profit organizations. The book's enduring relevance stems from its focus on timeless principles of disciplined leadership, strategic focus, and organizational culture rather than trendy management fads.

The work demonstrates that greatness is not primarily a function of circumstance but rather a matter of conscious choice and disciplined action, making it accessible to organizations willing to commit to the long-term journey from good to great.

The author makes a compelling case that the key to breakthrough problem-solving lies not in becoming better at finding solutions, but in becoming better at finding the right problems to solve.

Rather than accepting problems as they're initially presented, Wedell-Wedellsborg advocates for a more thoughtful, deliberate process of problem definition. Wedell-Wedellsborg emphasizes the importance of involving diverse perspectives in problem definition. The book's significance lies in its potential to transform how individuals and organizations approach their most challenging issues.
